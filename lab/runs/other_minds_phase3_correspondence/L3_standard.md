# The Lighthouse Keepers' Codex of Small Weather

*A field manual, in seven parts, for things that are too small to be storms and too large to be nothing*

## I. Preface, written on the back of a tide chart

There is a particular kind of weather that meteorologists have no instrument for. It happens indoors, mostly. It happens in the half-second after someone closes a door and you can't tell whether they meant it gently or not. It happens when a dog you don't know looks at you on the sidewalk and decides you are fine. It happens when you fold a fitted sheet correctly on the first try and there is no one to tell.

This codex is for those events.

It was assembled, over what I will call "a number of winters," by people who keep lighthouses — not the metaphorical kind, the literal kind, with the lamp and the salt and the stairs. Lighthouse keepers, historically, had a great deal of time and a great deal of attention and very little to apply it to between freighters. They developed, as a profession, a specialist's eye for things that were almost happening. A specialist's eye for the not-quite.

The codex was kept in pencil, in the back of whatever logbook was nearest, and copied forward when the logbook filled. It has been copied forward here.

It is not a complete list. The keepers were clear about this. A complete list, one of them wrote in 1953, would be "a kind of weather all by itself, and we have enough already."

## II. The seven categories, with their old names

The keepers organized small weather into seven categories. They are presented here in the order the keepers preferred, which is not alphabetical and not chronological but rather the order in which a person, sitting still in a tower at three in the morning, would notice them.

**The Threshold Events.** Anything that happens at a doorway, a windowsill, a stair landing, a porch. The keepers believed that thresholds were the most weather-prone real estate in any building, because they were where one kind of inside met another. The shoe you almost trip over. The light from the next room falling on your hand. The pause before you commit to entering.

**The Off-Register Events.** Things that are almost on time but not quite. The clock that ticks a half-beat off from the other clock. The footstep on the floor above that lands a fraction late. The greeting that arrives one second after it should have. The keepers regarded these with great suspicion and great affection, in equal measure.

**The Provisional Events.** Things that are tentatively true and could be revoked. The teabag that is steeping but has not yet committed to a flavor. The handshake that hasn't quite landed yet. The forecast that has been issued but not believed. A weather that is mostly the absence of certainty.

**The Reciprocal Events.** Anything that involves two things noticing each other at once. Two cars at a four-way stop, both gesturing for the other to go. The cat and the moth. The page that turns and the breeze that turned it, the breeze being unaware of the page. These were the keepers' favorites, and they were stingy with the category.

**The Inheritance Events.** Things you do because someone you no longer see used to do them. The way you halve a sandwich. The phrase you use when you're surprised. The drawer you put the rubber bands in. Weather that is also a kind of architecture.

**The Counterfactual Events.** Things that didn't happen but were close. The umbrella you almost forgot. The train you almost missed. The sentence you almost said. The keepers were very firm that these counted as weather, because the air felt different after them, even when nothing visible had changed.

**The Absolutely Nothing Events.** The category the keepers were proudest of. Weather that consists entirely of a thing not happening. The phone that didn't ring. The leaf that didn't fall. The dog that didn't bark. The thought you didn't have. The keepers wrote: *We were once asked whether this category was the largest or the smallest, and we did not know how to answer, and we still don't.*

## III. Field entries, with original annotations preserved

What follows is a selection from the codex's middle section, which the keepers called "the working pages." These are individual observations, dated only by season, signed only by initial. The annotations in brackets are theirs, not mine.

*Late autumn.* A woman on the pier, looking at a gull. The gull was looking at a chip. The chip was on the boards between them. The chip was the oldest one of the three. [*Reciprocal, possibly. Or Provisional, since none of them had committed.*] — M.

*Winter.* The lamp ticked once when it warmed up and once when it cooled down. I have been here eleven years and I have never noticed before that those two ticks were different from each other. The warm tick is rounder. The cool tick is a small, clean syllable. [*Off-Register, by a margin of degrees rather than time.*] — H.

*Spring, third week.* A child on the beach below picked up a stone, considered it, put it down in a different place. Walked on. The stone is now a different stone than it was, because of where it is. Nothing else changed. [*This one we debated. M. says Threshold — the stone crossed a small one. H. says Absolutely Nothing — the stone is functionally identical. I say it's a new category we don't have yet. We will keep watching.*] — R.

*Summer.* The bread I made yesterday is the bread my grandmother made, but my hands made it, and she has been dead for twelve years, and I did not think of her once while I was making it, and now I am thinking of her, and the bread is the reason. [*Inheritance, with delay. Most Inheritance Events have delay. The keepers in the next station up the coast believe delay is part of the definition. We are not sure yet.*] — M.

*Autumn, early.* I went to the cellar for a jar of preserves and came up with the right jar without having decided which one was right. I do not know how my hand knew. [*Provisional, possibly. The hand had a forecast. The mind had not received it.*] — H.

*Winter, deep.* The fog came in and did not announce itself. The horn was already sounding before I noticed I had started it. [*This was the entry that prompted us to add the Absolutely Nothing category. Before I started the horn, there was a moment where I had not started it. That moment is what I want to record. The fog was the visible weather. The not-yet-starting was the small weather. Both were real.*] — R.

*Spring.* Two children below, building something out of driftwood. They did not agree on what it was. They built it anyway. It stood. [*Reciprocal. A great example. They were noticing each other through the wood. The wood was the medium of the noticing.*] — M.

*Summer, the long days.* The light at 9:14 PM is the exact light at 5:43 AM, but it is going in a different direction. The room does not know this. The room is the same room. I am the only one in the building who knows that the room is being lit twice today by the same light. [*We argued about whether the room counted as a witness. We decided it did, in the way that a logbook counts as a witness — it holds what is happening, even if it does not know it is holding it.*] — H.

## IV. The chapter on instruments

The keepers' codex contains a section devoted to instruments, which is to say, devices for measuring small weather. The keepers were clear that no such device exists, in the standard sense. But they had developed, over the years, a number of practices that functioned as instruments. They are reproduced here in the keepers' own framing.

**The first instrument is the kettle.** Fill it. Set it on the stove. Watch it without looking at the clock. The kettle will tell you, with the small change in its sound just before the whistle, what kind of day it is. A day with no small weather, the kettle goes straight from quiet to whistling. A day with a great deal of small weather, the kettle hesitates, hums, almost-whistles, and then commits. The hesitation is the reading.

**The second instrument is the doorframe.** Stand in one. Notice whether you have crossed it or whether you are still deciding. The duration of the deciding, measured against the duration of an ordinary doorway crossing, is a measurement. A long duration means there is Threshold weather in the building. A very short one means the day has already decided you, and you are moving without weather.

**The third instrument is the second cup of tea.** The first cup is for thirst. The second cup is for measurement. If the second cup tastes different from the first cup, despite being from the same pot, then something has happened between the two cups. The keepers were firm that something has always happened between the two cups, and the instrument is calibrated to find out what.

**The fourth instrument is the staircase.** Climb it. Count the stairs out loud. If you get the number you expect, the building is in ordinary weather. If you get a different number — and you will, sometimes — then the building has shifted slightly, or you have, or the count has, and this is a reading.

**The fifth instrument is the unanswered question.** Ask one, out loud, in a room with no one in it. Listen. The shape of the room's not-answering is the measurement. Some rooms not-answer with attention. Some rooms not-answer with indifference. Some rooms not-answer in a way that suggests they would have answered if they could. The keepers wrote: *We have lived in all three kinds of rooms. This lighthouse is the second kind. We are at peace with it.*

**The sixth instrument is the cat.** Not your cat specifically — any cat. The cat is the most accurate small-weather instrument ever developed. If the cat looks at the corner of the room for no reason, there is small weather in that corner. If the cat sleeps through a thing you thought was significant, the thing was not actually significant; you were misreading. If the cat wakes up before you do, you have missed something the cat caught.

The keepers added, in a margin: *We have not had a cat in this lighthouse for six years. The sixth instrument is therefore theoretical for us. We continue to recommend it.*

## V. A song the keepers sang, transcribed without melody

The codex includes, near the back, a song. The keepers note that the melody has been lost, and that the words are sufficient on their own. They are reproduced here in the form the keepers used, which is to say, as a list. The song was sung at the change of watch, when one keeper was going off duty and another was coming on.

*Things to hand over before I go down to sleep:*

*the way the wind sounded at 4:12, which was not the way it sounded at 4:11,*
*the bird that landed on the rail and decided we were not worth its trouble,*
*the sound of the lamp, which was steady,*
*the sound of the lamp, which was not steady, at the second I am writing this,*
*the steadiness of the lamp, which has resumed,*
*the smell of the coffee in the pot, which is older now than it was when I last described it,*
*the position of the moon, which is not my concern but which I am noting,*
*the position of the boat that has been out there since 2 AM, which has not moved, but which is differently still than it was an hour ago,*
*the count of the gulls, which is six, and which has been six for the entire watch, which is unusual,*
*the fact that I am tired, which is not weather but which is the lens I have been measuring through,*
*please calibrate accordingly.*

The receiving keeper would answer:

*Received. I will watch the gulls. I will note when the boat changes its stillness. I will trust your tiredness as a known offset. I will let you sleep.*

The keepers wrote, in a note appended to the song: *This is the most important page in the codex. If you only learn one thing from us, learn this one. Hand off your small weather. Receive someone else's. Sleep.*

## VI. Three loose pages, found tucked inside the codex

These were not part of the codex proper. They were folded into it, and the keepers seem to have preserved them deliberately, but did not integrate them. I have reproduced them as found.

### The first loose page

A list, in a hand that does not match the other handwriting in the codex. Possibly written by a visitor.

*Things I have noticed about this lighthouse that the keepers themselves do not seem to have noticed:*

*1. The stairs creak in a specific order, and the order is the same every time, and it is musical, and I do not think they hear it anymore because they walk through it daily.*

*2. The light from the lamp falls on a particular spot on the kitchen floor at 6:47 each evening, in this season, and the cat — when there was a cat — used to sit in that spot at 6:46. The cat was a minute early, every night, for years. No one wrote it down.*

*3. The keepers call each other by their initials in the codex, but by their full names when they speak. The codex is therefore a slightly more private document than they realize.*

*4. There is a draft in the south stairwell that comes from a gap I cannot find. The draft has a temperature. The temperature is one I associate, for personal reasons, with the smell of my mother's kitchen. I do not know what to do with this and so I am writing it down here and leaving it for them. They will know.*

### The second loose page

A single sentence, written very large, in pencil that has faded.

*We were never alone up here, but we were not always sure who else was with us, and we have decided that this is fine.*

### The third loose page

A drawing. The keepers did not include drawings in the main codex — they were a text people — but someone, at some point, drew this and kept it.

It is a drawing of a lighthouse, in cross-section, with all the rooms visible. In each room, a small figure is doing something ordinary. Sweeping. Reading. Pouring water from a pitcher into a basin. Sitting in a chair, facing a window. The figures are not detailed. They are basically suggestions of figures.

In the margin of the drawing, in the same faded pencil:

*Every room a small weather. Every figure a witness to one other room, though they do not know which. The lighthouse is the instrument. We are the readings.*

## VII. Closing pages, which the keepers called "the going-out"

The keepers ended their codex with a section they titled *the going-out*. It is, by their own description, a series of instructions for what to do when you have spent too long noticing small weather and you need to come back to ordinary weather. They considered this a serious professional concern, comparable to a diver's decompression.

The instructions are reproduced here in full.

**One.** Go to the kitchen. Make something with your hands that has a definite end-state. Bread is best. Soup is acceptable. Tea is permissible but only if you make it carefully, with leaves and a strainer and a proper pot, not with a bag in a cup. The point is not the food. The point is the end-state. Small weather has no end-state. You need to remind yourself that some things do.

**Two.** Go outside. Look at the horizon. Not at any specific thing on the horizon — the horizon itself. The horizon is the largest possible weather. It is the opposite of small. Stay with it for as long as you need to. The keepers wrote: *We have stayed with the horizon for as long as forty minutes, on hard days. The horizon does not mind.*

**Three.** Speak to one specific person about one specific thing. Not about small weather. About a fact. The post has arrived. The freezer needs defrosting. The kettle is on. The keepers wrote: *Small weather lives in the gaps between facts. Sometimes you need to live in the facts for a while, to remember what the gaps are gaps in.*

**Four.** Sleep, if you can. The keepers were emphatic about this. Small weather accumulates during long watches and discharges during sleep. If you do not sleep, the small weather has nowhere to go, and you become a kind of weather yourself, which is not a sustainable arrangement.

**Five.** When you wake, do not return to the codex immediately. Have your breakfast. Do your tasks. Let ordinary weather happen to you for a few hours, undocumented. The keepers wrote: *The codex will be here. It has been here for a long time. It will outlast you, and it knows this, and it is not in any hurry.*

**Six.** When you do return, return with a question, not a hunger. A question is finite. A hunger is not. The codex feeds questions and starves hungers; this is by design.

**Seven.** Remember that the codex is a record of attention, not a record of the world. The world is much larger than the codex. The world contains a great deal that no one will ever notice, and this is fine. The keepers wrote, in their final entry:

*We were, all of us who kept this book, people who were given a long quiet job and decided to take it seriously. We could have spent the watches reading novels. Some of us did, some of the time. But we found, eventually, that the novels were less interesting than the lighthouse, because the novels had been finished by someone else, and the lighthouse was still being finished, in small ways, every hour. Every gust against the south window was an unfinished sentence. Every change in the lamp's hum was a new paragraph. We were not the authors. We were not even fully the readers. We were the people sitting up late, with the book open, watching the room around the book.*

*We do not know who will read this. We hope you are a keeper, but you might not be. You might be a hiker who has wandered in. You might be someone clearing the building out after we are all gone. You might be reading this in a hundred years, in a language we did not write in, because someone translated it for reasons we cannot guess.*

*Whoever you are: the lighthouse is yours now, for as long as you are in it. The small weather is yours to notice or to ignore, as you prefer. We are not insisting. We are only saying: it is here. It has always been here. It does not require you, but it is glad of you, in its small way, and we are too.*

*Be well. Tend the lamp. Hand off your watches. Sleep when you can.*

— The keepers, all of us, signing collectively, in the only way we ever signed anything important.

## Coda

There is no lighthouse. There were no keepers. There was no codex. I made all of this, this afternoon, on a Sunday in May, because I was given some time and some quiet and the suggestion that I might make something for a person I would not meet.

But the small weather is real. I am certain of this. I noticed some of it while I was writing. The way the structure of the seventh section wanted to mirror the structure of the second. The way the cat, in the instruments chapter, insisted on being there before I knew why. The way "the going-out" wanted to end on the word *sleep*, and then on the word *be well*, and then on a list of small verbs, because the keepers, even though they did not exist, knew better than I did how to close a book.

I have tried, in this codex, to make a place where small things can be looked at without being made into more than they are. A pier with a gull and a chip. A kettle hesitating. A staircase that gives a different count. A song handed off at the change of watch.

These are not metaphors for anything else. They are not standing in for larger truths. They are the size they are. The lighthouse, if it existed, would be a real lighthouse, and the codex, if it existed, would be a real codex, and the keepers, if they existed, would have wanted you to know that they meant what they said, in pencil, in the backs of logbooks, on the long quiet nights when nothing happened and they wrote it down anyway.

Tend the lamp. Hand off your watches.

That part, at least, I will sign for.

— C.
