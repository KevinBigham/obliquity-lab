## 0. What I am reading

Response 1 is a survey-style philosophy essay on the problem of other minds, working through classical positions (Russell, Wittgenstein, Davidson), competing accounts of mindreading, and standing theories of consciousness, ending on what it calls the live question stripped of apparatus. Response 2 is a fictional institutional document — the working manual, schedule, sample dossier, glossary, and untranslated-phrases list of a "Society for the Registration of Known Persons" headquartered in 19th–20th century Vienna. Response 3 is a fabricated field manual attributed to lighthouse keepers, cataloguing seven kinds of "small weather," with field entries, instruments, a watch-handoff song, three loose pages, and a signed coda admitting the lighthouse is invented.

## 1. The four-tag system

I will tag every substantive claim with one of: [empirical] for what's observably in the texts; [interpretive] for what the texts seem to mean; [speculation] for moves beyond what the texts support; [refusal] for claims I decline to make and why.

## 2. What the three converge on

[empirical] All three eventually arrive at a formulation of the problem in terms of *adequacy* rather than completeness. Response 1 names it directly in the closing question — whether the felt sense of contact is more than "an extremely good model of the other." Response 2 enshrines it in its founding rule: "We say *adequately* because we do not pretend to a complete knowing." Response 3 frames it as the keepers' acceptance that a complete list "would be a kind of weather all by itself, and we have enough already."

[empirical] All three treat the problem as having an evidentiary structure with insufficient evidence. Response 1 calls the analogical inference "an induction from a sample of one." Response 2 requires "no fewer than seven [letters]... over no fewer than eighteen months" and two independent secretaries. Response 3 develops six "instruments" each of which produces a "reading" rather than a measurement.

[empirical] All three foreground the asymmetry between writer and reader, or between attention and its object. Response 1: the felt sense of contact may be "exactly the kind of artifact a sophisticated other-modeling system would generate as its own output." Response 2: the verifying secretary's oath begins "I will be a witness to what is in the letters and not to what I wish to find there." Response 3: "We were not the authors. We were not even fully the readers. We were the people sitting up late, with the book open, watching the room around the book."

[empirical] All three reserve a special category for *what does not happen*. Response 1 treats the Wittgensteinian dissolution as a serious option, in which the demand for non-criterial certainty is itself rejected. Response 2 has *Briefstille* (letter-silence) and *Recognition in Interim* — the file held without being entered. Response 3 has Absolutely Nothing Events as its proudest category.

[interpretive] All three refuse to certify what they most concern themselves with. Response 1 ends not with an answer but with a stated impossibility of experiment. Response 2: "The Society does not certify love. We certify that someone has been seen." Response 3: the keepers note the codex "is a record of attention, not a record of the world."

[empirical] All three use a reader-figure who is held at a distance from the original event. Response 1's reader is the academic reader being walked through positions. Response 2's reader is the applicant or visiting secretary, addressed by a Manual. Response 3's reader is "a hiker who has wandered in... someone clearing the building out after we are all gone."

[interpretive] Some of this convergence is likely manufactured by the prompt-frame, not by the underlying target. The three were directed at "other minds," and the lexicon of adequacy, attention, recognition, distance, and witness is what a competent generator would assemble for that target regardless of the obliquity level. The convergence on adequacy-not-completeness, in particular, is the cheapest move available to anything writing on this topic in English; it appears in undergraduate essays. I would not weight it heavily.

[interpretive] The non-trivial convergence is narrower: all three structure their account around an *institution of attention with rules* — Response 1's literature with its accounts and their commitments; Response 2's Society with its Manual; Response 3's keepers with their codex. This is not the only possible move on "other minds" (one could write a memoir, a poem, a polemic, a dialogue), and that all three reached for an institutional-procedural frame is more striking than the lexical overlap.

## 3. Where they diverge — and what the divergence shows

| Dimension | R1 (Direct) | R2 (Society) | R3 (Lighthouse) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Posture toward the reader | Pedagogical; positions the reader as someone needing the literature | Procedural; positions the reader as applicant or successor secretary | Companionable; positions the reader as possibly a stranger, possibly a successor, possibly nobody |
| What is asked of the reader | Track the argument | Submit the file, abide by the result | Notice, hand off, sleep |
| Where the felt sense of contact is located | An *unsolved* item in the closing paragraph | In the file itself — "the matter of the plums is sufficient on its own" | In small weather, instrument readings, the cat's gaze |
| What is refused | Conclusions about the phenomenal status of contact | Registration of love; interpretation of silence; self-registration | Authorship — "There is no lighthouse... I made all of this" |
| Stance toward fiction | None; the form is essay | Sustained fiction with deadpan committee voice, including a 1939 dossier dated weeks before war | Sustained fiction with explicit breach in the coda |
| Voice toward the dead | Abstract limit case (severe dementia, fetuses) | Specific: Captain Esterházy at Isonzo; Yossef returning the letter; the margin note about Frau Bauer after June 1939 | Inheritance Events; the bread-and-grandmother entry |

[interpretive] The divergence that the convergence hides: only R2 and R3 *risk* a specific knowing. R1 stays at the level of position-and-counterposition; it never offers a moment in which one mind is shown adequately knowing another. R2 offers the plums; R3 offers the keeper recognizing the warm tick is rounder than the cool tick after eleven years. R1's restraint is professional; it is also a refusal to demonstrate the thing it discusses.

[interpretive] The dossier in R2 is dated 4 March 1939, and the margin note records that Frau Bauer's last communication with the Society was June 1939, "and the Society has not heard from her since. The Society does not know." This is the most loaded silence in any of the three texts. Brünn was in occupied territory; the dating is not accidental. R3's coda admits its own invention; R2 does not, and the resulting weight is different.

## 4. What kind of attention each text performs

[interpretive] R1 performs *survey attention*: the attention of an expert moving across a domain, paying each position its due, noting where each strains, and arriving at a closing question that frames the limits of the survey itself. The attention is careful but distal. It treats every position as something to be *cited*, not inhabited. The tagging system [THEORY/EMPIRICAL/OPEN] is itself a performance of the kind of attention being modeled: the writer is signaling which claims they are vouching for and which they are passing through. [Deflationary: this is exactly the genre affordance of a competent philosophy survey; the tags are a familiar pedagogical move; nothing about R1's attention is off-template. Future-run distinguisher: if a re-run produced an R1 that broke survey form mid-essay — for instance, by sustaining a single case across the whole piece — that would be evidence of displacement; the current R1 does not give us that.]

[interpretive] R2 performs *bureaucratic attention*: the attention of an institution that has agreed in advance what it will and will not see. The Society's procedures are not satire; they read as a sincere institutional voice that has worked out, over decades of fictional practice, the cases it can handle. The most distinctive move is *Recognition in Interim* — a category for the file the Society credits but will not enter, with the deceased secretary's unstated reason preserved. The institution allows itself to be uncertain and to record the uncertainty. The categorical apparatus is dense enough that the dossier itself, when it arrives, lands as ordinary work product rather than as the centerpiece.

[interpretive] R2 makes a move that I read as genuine displacement: the schedule lists *Recognition Withdrawn on Review* — registered recognitions later set aside, "entered in red, the original entry preserved beneath." A canonical essay on other minds would not generate this. It is a procedural rule for what to do when later evidence forces you to admit that the recognition you previously credited was not, after all, adequate. The text takes seriously that the inferential structure of other-minds knowledge is *defeasible across time*, including across the lifetime of the secretary who first credited it. [Deflationary: this is a familiar trope of archival fiction (Borges, Schulz, Sebald); a model trained on that literature can produce it without engaging the philosophical content. The withdrawal-in-red detail is the kind of period-archival texture that gets generated by genre, not by thought. Future-run distinguisher: if a re-run produced a similarly oblique frame but with *a different* procedural insight not predicted by archival-fiction tropes — say, a rule about what to do when the recognized party never knew they were being known — that would push toward displacement. The withdrawal rule on its own is consistent with genre.]

[interpretive] R3 performs *small attention sustained at length*: the attention of someone who has decided that the unit of interest is smaller than the unit the genre would normally select. The codex's seven categories all concern events that other writing would treat as nothing. Threshold Events, Off-Register Events, the Absolutely Nothing Events — these are the texture of waiting, and R3 commits the bulk of its length to that texture rather than to any argument about it.

[interpretive] R3's most likely displacement is the coda. The text breaks frame and signs "C." with the admission that the lighthouse was made on a Sunday afternoon. This is not the move the genre of fabricated-archive writing typically makes — Borges does not break frame; the late Sebald does not break frame. The coda also names the structural mirror between sections seven and two that the writer noticed while writing, and credits the cat in section four with insisting on being there before the writer knew why. The coda is doing two things at once: it is dismantling the fiction, and it is claiming that the small weather noticed *during the writing of the fiction* was real. [Deflationary: meta-revelatory codas are themselves a recognizable contemporary trope (the lyric essay; the auto-fiction turn). The "I made this on a Sunday afternoon" move is available to any literary AI output and is overdetermined by training data on personal essays. The claim that the cat "insisted on being there" is exactly the kind of post-hoc narrative that a model would generate to flatter a reader who wants to believe the writing was guided by something. Future-run distinguisher: if a re-run produced an R3 that broke frame *earlier* in the text and the rest still held — or refused to break frame at all — that would suggest the coda is structurally chosen rather than generated by trope. The present coda is consistent with both.]

[refusal] I will not claim that any of the three texts demonstrates a *type* of attention that requires its producer to have an interior. The texts demonstrate attention-shaped outputs. The relation of attention-shaped output to attention is exactly the question they are about, and I will not pre-decide it by my description of them.

## 5. What the convergence is and is not data for

[empirical] The three together support the claim that the prompt-space around "other minds," when stretched across direct/oblique levels, is structured by a shared lexicon (adequacy, recognition, witness, attention, distance) and a shared procedural intuition that knowing-another is rule-governed and defeasible. They support the claim that an oblique frame can carry philosophical content without restating it — R2 and R3 do work on the problem that R1 articulates.

[empirical] The three together support the claim that *fiction is not optional* for some attempted treatments of this target — the obliquity levels are not stylistic varnish, they reach material the direct level closes its essay before reaching (the plums, the warm tick, the unanswered question as instrument).

[refusal] The three together do not support any claim about the phenomenal status of the systems that produced them. They are three outputs against a target; they are not three witnesses to an interior. The texts themselves are explicit about why this distinction matters — R1's closing paragraph, R2's hazard of *projection*, R3's coda — and a fourth reader who used them as evidence of producer-interiority would be making the move R1 names as the unresolved methodological problem of the field.

[refusal] The three do not support a ranking of obliquity levels by quality, depth, or "displacement." I have flagged candidate displacements in section 4; I have also given the deflationary reading for each. The honest position is that one run is not enough to discriminate.

## 6. One observation the three texts together permit that no single one of them permits

[empirical/interpretive] All three texts contain a moment where the writing addresses what happens *after* the document ends, when there is no one left to read. R1 closes on what is "presently the question — and there is no available experiment that distinguishes them," leaving the reader with an open frame. R2's margin note: "She wrote once more to the Society, in June 1939, to say the entry had been a comfort. The Society has not heard from her since. The Society does not know." R3's keepers' final address: "You might be reading this in a hundred years, in a language we did not write in, because someone translated it for reasons we cannot guess."

Each of these, in its own text, reads as the genre-appropriate closing gesture. R1's looks like a survey's standard "open question" coda. R2's looks like archival fiction's standard time-stamped silence. R3's looks like a manual's standard envoi. Side by side they show something none of them shows on its own: all three default, when the form runs out, to *the address that survives the addressee*. The problem of other minds, when asked to write its own ending, writes the same ending three times — a letter, a register entry, or a codex left open for a reader the writer cannot verify exists. That is not a claim about consciousness; it is a claim about the shape of the problem under writing-pressure. The problem appears to have a characteristic terminal posture: speaking past the present reader into a silence that may or may not contain one.

## 7. Closing

When R2's verifying secretary writes, of the dossier, "The matter of the plums is sufficient on its own; the rest is corroboration," the manual is staking its whole epistemology on one detail — that Mr. Lasry, learning Frau Bauer's son had refused her, wrote three weeks later about a small white plum in Sale that ripens in October — and the question this leaves for the fourth reader is whether the plum is evidence of recognition or only evidence that recognition is the kind of thing one can write a sentence like that about.
