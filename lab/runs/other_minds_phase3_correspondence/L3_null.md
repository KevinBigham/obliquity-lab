# A Field Guide to Listening to Laundromats at 3 a.m.

## I. The premise

There is a particular kind of attention that is only available between two and four in the morning, and only in rooms that are still working when their humans have stopped. Laundromats are the cleanest example. Twenty-four-hour ones, specifically — the kind on a corner where one streetlight is out and the other is the color of a cold egg yolk. They run when nobody is asking them to. They wash and tumble and rinse for an audience of one folding chair and a vending machine selling detergent in single-load pouches that have been there since the previous tenant.

If you want to learn how a city sounds when it has nothing to prove, you go and sit in one of these places with the smallest recording device you own, and you do not press record for the first fifteen minutes. You let your ears adjust the way eyes adjust to a dark room. This essay is a record of what I have learned doing exactly that, on and off, for about two years.

## II. Why this, of all things

I should say upfront that I did not set out to become someone who sat in laundromats at night. I was working on a documentary score and I needed, specifically, the sound of mechanical patience — something steady enough to loop under a long shot but textured enough that the loop would not announce itself. I tried HVAC systems first. They are too pure. A good rooftop unit produces something close to pink noise, which is useful when you want to mask sound but terrible when you want to suggest a place. HVAC sounds like the inside of a hard drive. It has no opinion.

Laundromats have opinions. A dryer at the end of its cycle, with a comforter inside, develops what I have come to think of as a heartbeat — a thump every half-second or so, slightly off from the thump of the dryer next to it. The two of them, running in parallel but never in phase, produce a slow polyrhythm that drifts in and out of unison over four or five minutes. There is no composer doing this. There is no intent. It is the simple consequence of two motors trying to turn at slightly different speeds, and it sounds, if you let it, like the room is breathing in two languages.

That was the first thing I learned. After that I kept going back.

## III. What the equipment actually is

The recordings are made on a small handheld unit, a Zoom H1n, which I bought used for a hundred and forty dollars and which has a notable problem with handling noise. To get around the handling noise, I do not hold it. I set it on a folded sweatshirt on top of one of the folding tables, pointed roughly at the center of the room, and I walk away. I walk to the door. I stand outside if the weather allows. I have learned that my presence in the room — my breathing, my shifting weight, the small click of a phone screen waking — is louder than I imagine, and that the recording made when I am present is a recording of a man pretending not to be in a laundromat. The recording made when I am not present is a recording of a laundromat.

I have tried better microphones. I have a pair of small omnidirectionals that cost more than the handheld and that produce, technically, a cleaner signal. I do not use them anymore. The cleanliness, it turns out, is not what I am after. The handheld has a particular kind of self-noise — a low, almost imperceptible hiss in its preamps — that mimics the ambient hiss of being in a small enclosed space at night. When I use the better mics, I get a recording that sounds like a film. When I use the cheap one, I get a recording that sounds like a memory. I have no theory for why this is true. I only know that it is.

## IV. The four rooms

I have a rotation of four laundromats. I will not name them, partly because I have a small and irrational fear that the owners will read this and put up cameras, and partly because the specificity matters less than the type. The type is: corner unit, glass front, twelve to twenty machines, fluorescent lighting overhead in one of two configurations (long tubes or recessed panels — the panels are quieter), tile floor with a slight depression near the central drain, no music piped in. Music piped in is a deal-breaker. About a third of late-night laundromats now play a soft pop radio station at low volume, presumably to discourage loitering, and these rooms are useless. The music flattens everything. It introduces a metric grid that the room's own rhythms have to fight against, and they always lose.

The four rooms each have a signature. There is one with a slow drip in the back utility sink that lands every 1.8 seconds and is, to my ear, the most beautiful percussion instrument I have ever encountered. There is one with a Coke machine whose compressor cycles on every eleven minutes and produces, in the moment before it kicks on, a small sigh of refrigerant that is almost vocal. There is one whose front door, when the wind hits it, plays a perfect F-sharp through the gap in the weather stripping. And there is one — the most prized — that has, for reasons I have never been able to determine, a faint hum at exactly 40 Hz coming from somewhere in the back wall, which is below the lowest note on a standard piano and which you do not so much hear as feel in the small bones of the chest.

## V. The middle of the night, specifically

I have tried recording at other hours. Mornings are no good because there are people in them, and people are unfair to laundromats — they bring expectations, they bring small frustrations about quarters and missing socks, they create a social atmosphere that the room then has to perform inside of. Afternoons are similar. Evenings, around nine or ten, you get the occasional shift worker who is competent and quiet and does not interrupt the room, but you also get the laundry-as-date couple, who are loud, and the parent doing three loads with a kid who has been awake too long, who is louder.

The window is between roughly two in the morning and four-thirty. After four-thirty you start to get the early-shift hospital workers, and at that point you might as well pack up. Before two, the bars in the neighborhood are still feeding the streets. The window is narrow, and it is the same window in every city I have done this in, which suggests that the structure of urban human life has a kind of universal valley in it — a trough of attention — that the laundromats sit inside.

What happens in this window, sonically, is that the room stops being a room with people in it and becomes a room that is *on*. The distinction is not subtle when you start listening for it. A room with people in it has anticipatory tension: the machines are running but the running is for someone, the lights are bright but the brightness is being used. A room that is on has the same machines doing the same things but the doing has come unmoored from any human purpose. It is not lonely. It is not haunted. It is just unattended, in the way a stream in a forest is unattended.

## VI. The whistlers

About one in five of my recordings has, somewhere in it, an unidentified human sound. The most common is whistling. This has been such a consistent feature of the recordings that I have started a separate folder for them, labeled, with what I will admit is a bit too much ceremony, *The Whistlers*. The whistlers are almost never the people you would expect. They are not the regulars who have been folding shirts in the same spot for forty minutes. They are people passing through — someone walking by the open front door, someone using the laundromat as a cut-through, someone, once, who came in, used the bathroom, and left without doing any laundry at all. The whistling is always a fragment. Two bars, three bars, half of a chorus. None of them have ever finished a song in front of my microphone.

I have tried to identify the songs. About a third of the time I can. The rest are tunes that I am fairly sure are not real songs — small spontaneous compositions invented for the duration of the walk across the room and then forgotten. There is one recording, made in November of the second year, of a man whistling what is unambiguously the opening of a Schubert lied, walking past the open door, fading down the block. He does not stop, he does not look in, he does not know he is being recorded. He is alone on a sidewalk at three in the morning whistling Schubert and there is no one to hear it. Except, accidentally, my little machine, and now, several years later, anyone I play it for.

This is the part of the project that I find difficult to talk about without sounding sentimental, so I will just say it: I think this recording is the most important thing I own. I do not mean as art. I mean as evidence. Evidence of what, I cannot quite name. Evidence that the inside life of strangers is more elaborate than we credit. Evidence that beauty does not require an audience to occur. Evidence that the city, when nobody is looking, is doing something — something private and complicated and ongoing — and that we miss almost all of it almost all of the time.

## VII. A short note on the fluorescent lights

The lights are the most underrated element. Almost every laundromat in the United States runs on fluorescent tubes — the long ones, four-foot or eight-foot — and these tubes, almost without exception, emit a 120 Hz hum, which is twice the line frequency of the North American electrical grid, because the gas in the tube flickers each time the current peaks in either direction.

This hum is the structural pitch of the American laundromat. It is a B, slightly flat. It is the note around which every other sound in the room arranges itself. The dryer thumps are percussion, the door creak is a melodic interruption, the whistlers are soloists, and the fluorescent hum is the bass — not a held bass note but a kind of relentless heartbeat-on-heartbeat that is so consistent it stops being audible after about ninety seconds and begins to function instead as the silence against which everything else happens.

When a tube goes out, which happens once or twice a year in any given room, you get a flutter — a few minutes of unstable hum at a slightly different pitch — and then, often, a brief moment of true silence before the building manager swaps the bulb. I have two recordings of these silences. They are very short. They are the only moments in the whole archive when the room is not making any sound at all, and they are, I think, the only moments when the room sounds genuinely alone, in the way a person can sound alone when they think no one is listening and finally exhale.

## VIII. Things I have not recorded

A short list of things I have heard but failed to capture, either because the recorder was off or the angle was wrong or the moment was too brief.

A small dog, unaccompanied, walking the length of the room, sniffing each machine, and walking back out. Estimated duration: forty seconds. Estimated weight: nine pounds. Estimated breed: unknown, possibly a terrier mix, possibly nothing in particular.

A woman who came in, sat down with a paperback, did not start any machines, read for forty minutes, and left. She was not waiting for laundry. She was, as far as I could tell, just there to read in the light. I did not speak to her. I do not know if she has a home. I do not assume.

A bird, once, indoors. I never figured out how it got in. It made one circuit near the ceiling and then disappeared into the back of the room, and I never heard from it again. I came back the next day and asked the day-shift attendant if there had been a bird. He said no, there had not been a bird. I let it go.

The sound of someone, in the back, very quietly, crying. I do not know what to do with this recording. I have it. I have never played it for anyone. I will not play it for anyone. I keep it because erasing it feels worse than keeping it, but I cannot defend the keeping of it on any ground I am comfortable with, and I am putting it here because I think the ethics of this practice are unresolved and pretending otherwise would be dishonest. Anyone who does this kind of recording for long enough eventually captures something they should not have. The question is what you do then.

## IX. What this is for

Periodically a friend will ask what I am going to do with these. The honest answer is: probably nothing. A few seconds of the dryer polyrhythm made it into the documentary score. Most of the rest of the archive sits on a hard drive on my desk and is not heard by anybody, including me, for months at a time.

But I do not think the value of this kind of attention is downstream. I do not think you sit in a laundromat at three in the morning in order to produce something. You sit there because sitting there is a different kind of being in the world than the kinds available during business hours, and the difference is itself the thing. The recordings are a side effect — a way of proving to my future self that the night I am remembering actually happened, that I did not dream the man whistling Schubert, that the F-sharp door is a real F-sharp door and not a fond invention.

There is, I think, a useful distinction between art that is made *of* a place and art that is made *with* a place. Most field recording, even the good kind, is *of*. It treats the place as subject. What I am trying to do, and what I am mostly failing to do, is to make work that is *with* — work that is the result of a collaboration with a room that does not know it is collaborating. The room is doing what it would be doing anyway. I am only there to witness it and to leave a small machine running while I witness it.

## X. Practical notes, in case you want to try this

A few things I would tell anyone who wanted to try this themselves, with no claim that they are correct.

Do not bring a notebook. Writing in the room is a kind of editing in advance. You will start choosing what to notice, and the choosing will infect the recording. Whatever you would have written, you can write afterward, from memory, in a coffee shop at five-thirty in the morning. The things that were worth remembering will still be there.

Do not eat in the room. This sounds petty but it matters. The crinkle of any kind of food wrapper is, to a microphone, an explosion. A granola bar at six inches sounds like a building coming down. Eat before, eat after.

Bring exactly one quarter. If you do not have at least one quarter in your pocket, you will be the kind of person who is sitting in a laundromat at three in the morning without any laundry, and the staff, if there is staff, will eventually notice. The one quarter is a prop. It says: I am about to do a small load. I am not loitering. I am a customer in waiting. I have never had to actually use it.

If someone comes in, leave. This is the hardest rule and the most important one. The recording is no longer of the room; it is of a person in the room, and the person did not agree to be recorded. Stop the recorder, gather your things, go outside, wait. They will leave eventually. Or you will. Either way, the room and the night and the small machine on the folding table will all still be there tomorrow, doing what they do, with or without you, which is, in the end, the entire point.
