# THE SOCIETY FOR THE REGISTRATION OF KNOWN PERSONS

*Founded at Trieste, 1872. Removed to Vienna, 1879. Records kept since.*

## I. WORKING MANUAL OF THE SOCIETY

*Being the Eleventh Edition, revised by the Chief Correspondent under the standing committee, with the original Preface preserved.*

### Preface of the Chief Correspondent

The Society was founded by Adelheid Vasari and four others in the autumn of 1872 for a purpose that we have not, in the intervening years, found cause to amend, though we have been obliged to refine our language for it. The purpose is the registration, by careful procedure, of the fact that one person has been adequately known by another across a distance neither of them has crossed.

We say *adequately* because we do not pretend to a complete knowing. We say *across a distance* because the matter would not arise otherwise; persons who meet have ordinary means of recognition, and our work is not needed there. We say *known* in the older sense, which is to say held in attention long enough that the attention has shape.

It has been suggested, more than once, that the Society's work is sentimental. The Society does not think so. A correspondence may run twenty years and produce nothing the Society can register; another may produce a recognition in the third letter. The matter is not affection. The matter is whether the correspondent has, by the evidence of the letters, come into the actual presence of the other as the other is, rather than as the correspondent had hoped or feared.

We do not certify love. We certify that someone has been seen.

— T. K. Mörsch, Chief Correspondent, on the occasion of the Eleventh Edition

### Classes of Recognition Acknowledged by the Society

The Society acknowledges seven classes, of which the first three are the most commonly registered:

1. **Formal Recognition** — the recognition entered after sustained correspondence and after the verifying secretary's review of the file. The standard case.
2. **Intimate Recognition** — recognition between correspondents whose letters do not concern any external matter; the letters are themselves the subject. Registered only on application by both parties.
3. **Accidental Recognition** — recognition that occurs in the course of a correspondence undertaken for another purpose (commercial, scholarly, condolent). The most frequent of the modern era.
4. **Recognition in Interim** — recognition the Society has reason to credit but will not register at present, pending further letters or pending a doubt held by the secretary.
5. **Recognition Refused** — the case in which a correspondent has been adequately known but declines to be registered as such. The Society honours all refusals without entering them.
6. **Recognition Held Posthumously** — the case in which one correspondent dies before registration but the file is otherwise complete. The Society registers these with the deceased's name and the date of last letter; no signature of acceptance is required from the deceased party, the file being its own evidence.
7. **Recognition Withdrawn on Review** — the rare case in which a previously registered recognition is, on later examination by a successor secretary, set aside. Withdrawals are entered in red and the original entry is not erased.

### Procedure for Entry into the Formal Register

A correspondence may be entered into the Formal Register upon application by either party, accompanied by the letters in original or in faithful copy, no fewer than seven in number, exchanged over no fewer than eighteen months. The file is given to a verifying secretary who is not personally acquainted with either party. The secretary reads the file in one sitting where possible and renders an opinion. If the opinion is favourable, a second secretary reads the file independently. Disagreement between the two is resolved by the Chief Correspondent, whose decision is final but not infallible; appeal lies only with time.

The Society does not require the parties to have met, and in most registered cases they have not.

### Standing Rules of Conduct for Correspondents

Correspondents of the Society are bound to the following:

To write to the person who is there, not to the person one has supposed. To set aside, on each new letter, the impression formed of the correspondent before that letter arrived. To answer what has been asked. To not require, of the correspondent, that they be more interesting than they are. To not require, of the correspondent, that they be less. To enclose, where useful, a small object or a copy of a small object, that the correspondent may have a thing in hand. To never write a letter the correspondent will receive on a day the writer has not considered.

### Hazards

The hazards of the Society's work are three in number and are named so that the correspondent may know them.

The first is *projection*. The correspondent supplies, from his own person, the features he wishes to find in the other. The letters in such a case have a curious self-sufficiency; they require nothing from the correspondent to be answered.

The second is *failed translation*. The correspondent writes in a language not native to one or both, and the letters mean less than they appear to mean. The verifying secretary is instructed to mark such files for cautious reading.

The third is *the refused address*. The correspondent writes to a person who does not consent to be the addressee of the letters being written. This is the gravest of the three, as the recognition, however accurate, has no rightful place to land.

### What the Society Does Not Do

The Society does not arrange correspondences. The Society does not write on behalf of either party. The Society does not publish its registers. The Society does not advise on the conduct of a correspondence in progress, though it will, on application, send a copy of the present Manual. The Society does not certify reconciliation, friendship, courtship, or the cessation of any of these. The Society does not interpret silence. The Society does not register itself.

## II. SCHEDULE OF THE KINDS OF RECOGNITION

*Being the working schedule used by verifying secretaries in their review of files. Not exhaustive. Revised at intervals.*

**Formal Recognition.** The standard registration. One person has been adequately known by another through letters; both parties consent to registration; the file shows the knowing. Examples: the file of Müller and Acquaviva (1891–1908); the file of Rosenstock and Ferrand (1923–1929); the present case, Bauer and Lasry (see Dossier 1147). Evidence: seven letters minimum, eighteen months minimum, two secretaries concurring.

**Intimate Recognition.** Recognition between correspondents whose letters concern nothing but the correspondence itself, or the inward life of the parties. Registered only on joint application. Examples: the file of Sister Beata of Innsbruck and Mr. Henley of Bath (1899–1904); the file of two anonymous parties known to the Society only as L. and M. (1934). Evidence: a marked absence of external subject. The letters do not refer to weather, news, or third persons. Some secretaries find this class easier to recognise than to describe.

**Accidental Recognition.** Recognition that arises in the course of a correspondence begun for another reason. The Society receives these most often from solicitors who corresponded with the relatives of estates, from physicians who corresponded with patients, and from booksellers who corresponded with subscribers. Examples: the file of Dr. Halbersam and his Algerian patient (1911–1913); the file of the bookseller Veith and his customer in Lemberg (1888–1894). Evidence: the original business of the correspondence becomes, at a discernible point in the file, the lesser part of the letters.

**Recognition in Interim.** The Society credits the recognition but holds the entry. There may be doubt; there may be a missing letter; there may be a secretary's reservation that cannot be set aside but cannot be made specific. Examples: presently in interim: thirty-one files. The oldest in interim is from 1894 and remains so because the secretary who held it gave no reason and is now deceased. Evidence: the secretary's note. The Society has resolved, after debate, that an interim is also a record.

**Recognition Refused.** The recognition has occurred, but one party declines to be registered. The Society does not enter the file but writes to the applicant a letter of acknowledgement, which the applicant may keep. Examples: not given. The Society does not name refusers. Evidence: a single signed line: *I do not wish to be registered.*

**Recognition Held Posthumously.** One correspondent has died before registration, but the file is complete. The Society enters the recognition on its own authority. Examples: the file of Captain Esterházy and the widow Soltész (1916–1917, ending with the Captain's death at Isonzo); see Dossier 1147 below. Evidence: a death certificate or its equivalent; the absence of any refusal in the deceased's letters.

**Recognition Withdrawn on Review.** A previously registered recognition that a successor secretary, on rereading, can no longer credit. The matter is solemn. Withdrawals are rare; in the Society's history there have been forty-one. Examples: not given. Evidence: the withdrawing secretary's signed reason, entered in red, the original entry preserved beneath.

## III. DOSSIER 1147 — BAUER AND LASRY

*Verifying secretary: F. Hollander. Second reader: I. Czerny. Class: Recognition Held Posthumously. Registered 4 March 1939.*

**Correspondents.** Ottilie Bauer, née Sandberg, of Brünn (Brno), seamstress and later, after the death of her husband, lodging-house keeper. Aron Lasry, of Sale, Morocco, dealer in copper kitchenware, later in spices, latterly retired.

**Origin of the correspondence.** A letter posted from Sale on 16 May 1924, addressed to Frau Bauer, returning a brooch. The brooch had belonged to Frau Bauer's husband's mother and had been lost on a sea passage in 1898; Mr. Lasry had purchased it from a sailor in 1899 and kept it, having read the inscription on the reverse (*K.B. an O.S., 1893*), with the intention of returning it should the owner ever be found. He found her, twenty-five years later, by an inquiry placed in a German trade journal his nephew sent him.

The brooch was received. Frau Bauer wrote to thank him on 4 June 1924. Mr. Lasry replied. The correspondence continued, in German on both sides (Mr. Lasry's German being learned in Tangier from a friend of his youth and being, by his own account, ungainly), until Mr. Lasry's death in November 1938.

**Letters in the file.** Sixty-four, of which forty-one are from Mr. Lasry and twenty-three from Frau Bauer. The disproportion is noted; Frau Bauer's letters were longer.

**Verifying secretary's notes** (F. Hollander, dated 1 February 1939).

The file is unusual in three respects. First, the parties never proposed to meet; both refer to the matter once, in 1927, and dismiss it without regret. Second, the correspondence has no second purpose; after the return of the brooch there is nothing to transact, and yet the letters continue for fourteen years. Third, the parties write in a language native to neither in the full sense; Frau Bauer's mother tongue was Czech though she wrote in German, and Mr. Lasry's German, as stated, was learned. The Society's standing caution as to failed translation applies and has been considered.

Notwithstanding the caution, I find the file shows recognition. Frau Bauer writes, in 1929, that her son has refused to take her into his household in Vienna and that she will not press him; Mr. Lasry replies, three weeks later, that one should not press a child who has decided the shape of his life, and that he is sorry, and that there is in Sale a kind of small white plum that ripens in October and that he will send some preserved if she will permit it. She permits it. The plums are sent. In 1932 Frau Bauer writes that her arthritis has come into her hands and that she has stopped sewing; Mr. Lasry replies that he too has stopped lifting the larger pots and that they are now both, he supposes, in the second of the lives a person is given. Frau Bauer writes back that she had not thought of it as a second life but accepts the description. From 1933 onwards Mr. Lasry's letters slow; Frau Bauer's do not. She writes to him about the lodgers (a Polish music-teacher; a young man from Pressburg who reads at the table); she writes to him about a cat that comes to her kitchen door; she writes to him, in 1937, that she has begun to keep his letters in a particular drawer and that she takes one out, some evenings, and reads it.

The last letter from Mr. Lasry is dated 9 October 1938. It is brief and concerns the weather at Sale and a recipe for a sweet made of almonds. Frau Bauer's next letter, dated 1 December 1938, was returned to her by Mr. Lasry's nephew Yossef, with a covering letter informing her of the death of his uncle on 14 November.

Frau Bauer applied to the Society on 11 January 1939, enclosing the file. She wrote: *I would like it entered. He would not have applied; he did not know of the Society; he would have said it was for finer people than us. But he knew me and I knew him and there should be a place where this is written down.*

I find the recognition. I recommend entry under the class of Recognition Held Posthumously.

— F. Hollander

**Second reader's note** (I. Czerny, dated 28 February 1939).

Concur. The matter of the plums is sufficient on its own; the rest is corroboration. Enter the file.

— I. Czerny

**Outcome.** Registered 4 March 1939. Frau Bauer was sent a copy of the entry and an acknowledgement of receipt of the file. The original letters were returned to her at her request.

*Margin note, in pencil, undated, in the hand of F. Hollander:* She wrote once more to the Society, in June 1939, to say the entry had been a comfort. The Society has not heard from her since. The Society does not know.

## IV. GLOSSARY FRAGMENT

*From the working glossary kept by the secretaries' room.*

**Adequacy.** The standard the Society applies. Not completeness. The correspondent need not have been fully known; only known truly, so far as the letters reach.

**The Address.** The person to whom a letter is in fact written, as distinct from the name on the envelope. In most cases these are the same. In some they are not, and the Society is concerned with the difference.

**Carriage.** The interval between the writing of a letter and its arrival. The Society holds that recognition occurs in the letters, not in the carriage, but that the carriage is not nothing.

**The Drawer.** Informal. The place a correspondent keeps the letters of another. A correspondent who has a drawer is, in the secretaries' usage, *drawered*; the term is not entered in the Manual but is common in the room.

**Entry.** The act of registering a recognition. Also the line in the register itself. *To enter a file* is the verifying secretary's verb.

**Held.** Of a recognition: credited but not entered. See *Recognition in Interim*. Of a letter: kept by the addressee in such a manner that it is referred to again. Of a person, in the older usage of the Society: known across distance and known still.

**The Interim.** The condition of a file the Society has not resolved. Plural: *interims*. There is some objection to the plural but it is used.

**Refusal.** A correspondent's decision not to be registered. To be distinguished from indifference; the Society does not register indifference either, but does not record it.

**The Return.** The case in which a letter comes back to its sender, the addressee being dead, removed, or no longer at the given direction. The Society has a particular regard for returns and keeps a separate small register of them, called *the Book of Returns*, which is not consulted except by the Chief Correspondent.

**Witness.** The verifying secretary, in the older usage. The word has fallen out of formal use but is preserved in the oath the secretaries take upon appointment, which begins *I will be a witness to what is in the letters and not to what I wish to find there.*

## V. THE LIST OF PHRASES FOR WHICH THE SOCIETY HAS NO SUFFICIENT ENGLISH

*Kept in the secretaries' room. Added to as needed. Not official.*

The Society conducts its work in German and corresponds in whatever language the file requires. The following phrases, encountered in files over the years, have resisted satisfactory translation into English, which is the language of the Society's external correspondence with British and American applicants. The list is kept so that secretaries new to the room may understand what is meant when an older colleague uses one of these in conversation.

*Nachträglich erkannt.* Recognised afterwards. Used of the case in which a correspondent realises, on rereading an earlier letter, that the other had already been adequately known at the time of writing and that the correspondent had not noticed. English *recognised in retrospect* is too neutral; the German carries a small reproach the secretary feels toward himself.

*Briefstille.* Letter-silence. The interval during which a correspondent does not write, neither having stopped nor continuing. To be distinguished from cessation. *Silence* alone will not do; the silence is specifically of letters, and is meaningful in a way that other silences are not.

*Doppelt adressiert.* Doubly addressed. Of a letter that is in fact written to two persons, only one of whom is named on the envelope. The second addressee may be the writer himself, or a third party, or the dead. English *written to two* is misleading; it suggests duplicity, which is not meant.

*Spätbrief.* The late letter. The letter that arrives after the recognition it would have produced is no longer possible. Most often, after the death of the addressee. Sometimes after a quarrel that cannot be mended. The English *too-late letter* sounds melodramatic; the German is merely descriptive.

*Erkenntnisweigerung.* The refusal of recognition. Specifically, the refusal by the correspondent to recognise the other when the evidence to do so is in his hand. To be distinguished from failure; the failure is honest. *Erkenntnisweigerung* is the case in which a correspondent has seen and declined to see.

*Halbbekannt.* Half-known. The condition of a correspondent of whom the file shows partial recognition, sufficient for affection but not for entry. English *partly known* will not do, as it suggests the part is the difficulty; the difficulty is that the half is the whole the correspondent has been willing to write.

*Ankommensgewiss.* Certain of arrival. Said of a letter that the writer knows will reach the addressee, in the older sense in which *arrival* meant not the post but the readiness of the other to receive. English has no word for this and the Society has tried several.

*Margin note, in a different hand:* We should give up on the English. The applicants who need these phrases will learn them.
