ARCHIVE OF THE DEPARTMENT OF DIRECTED REGARD
I. Operating Manual Excerpt (Rev. 1988)
Foreword by the Chief Registrar
The Department of Directed Regard was established in 1887 by an act of the Municipal Council following the Great Assessment. Our mandate was then, as it remains today, the formal cataloguing, measurement, and certification of human attention. We record where the eye, the ear, and the posture settle.
Over the past century, the Department has formalized the understanding that attention is not merely a biological reflex, but a civic resource, a transferable asset, and an observable mass. We measure the weight of a gaze upon a public square. We certify the vigilance paid to a deteriorating threshold. We acknowledge the physical labor of ignoring a given locus.
The Department does not ask why a citizen looks at a thing. The Department has stopped asking what the looking means. We concern ourselves exclusively with the duration, the trajectory, and the biological anchoring of the focus. The manual that follows outlines our operating procedures. It is to be adhered to without variation.
Formal Classes of Attention
The Department officially recognizes six primary classifications of attention:

1. Sustained Regard: The unbroken, deliberate visual or auditory focus upon a specific, localized object or area.
2. Ambient Vigilance: A low-grade, constant, and peripheral readiness directed toward a specific spatial perimeter or environmental condition.
3. Inherited Focus: Attention passed from one individual to a successor, consciously or unconsciously, directing focus toward a historical locus.
4. Aversive Deflection: The active, measurable physical effort required to continuously exclude a specific object, person, or area from one's sensory field.
5. Granular Fixation: The narrowing of focus upon the minute, structural details of a mundane object, resulting in the functional disappearance of the object's greater context.
6. Terminal Observation: The final, uninterrupted duration of attention given to an object, location, or living subject prior to its permanent departure or cessation.
Verification Procedures
To grant formal recognition to a claimed instance of attention, Field Assessors must complete the following protocol:

1. Initial Petition Review: The Assessor confirms the locus (the object of attention) is physical and stationary.
2. Baseline Establishment: The Assessor observes the petitioner in a neutral environment to determine their standard respiration and blink rates.
3. Blind Observation: The Assessor records the petitioner interacting with the locus for a minimum of 72 non-consecutive hours. The Assessor must remain entirely outside the petitioner's sensory field.
4. Fracture Testing: The Assessor introduces controlled, mild environmental stimuli (e.g., the dropping of a two-ounce steel ball at a distance of twenty feet) to measure the threshold at which the petitioner's attention breaks.
5. Chamber Review: Field logs are submitted to the Registry Chamber for final certification.
Standing Rules of Staff Conduct
Field Assessors will wear garments of matte grey or muted brown to minimize environmental disruption. Assessors are prohibited from wearing scented soaps, colognes, or noisy footwear. Under no circumstances is an Assessor to initiate eye contact with a petitioner while a session of Sustained Regard or Terminal Observation is underway. If an Assessor's presence fractures a petitioner's focus, the day's observation log is voided and must be destroyed.
Hazards of the Assessment Protocol
The work of the Department carries occupational risks. Assessors must monitor themselves for Somatic Mirroring, a hazard wherein the Assessor inadvertently matches the breathing and blink rates of the petitioner being observed. If left unchecked, this leads to severe cardiovascular fatigue. Assessors are also warned against Focal Capture, an occupational hazard occurring when the Assessor's own attention is permanently arrested by the petitioner's locus, rendering the Assessor unable to complete their objective documentation. Assessors experiencing unprompted recall of a petitioner's locus while off-duty must report to the Medical Wing immediately.
Exclusions of the Department
The Department registers the act of attention. It does not evaluate the worthiness of the object being attended to. The Department does not register reciprocal attention between two individuals; the mutual exchange of gaze is an issue of domestic relations, not civic registry. The Department does not diagnose or cure distraction. The Department does not know where attention goes when the locus is destroyed, and the Department has stopped asking.
II. Official Schedule of Classifications
The following taxonomy dictates the definitions, examples, and measurement conventions for the formal classes of attention recognized by the Department of Directed Regard.
Class I: Sustained Regard

* Definition: The continuous, primary application of sensory focus upon a defined physical locus.
* Examples: A machinist watching the lathe; a citizen tracking the movement of a cloud over a municipal building; a child watching a television broadcast.
* Measurement Convention: Quantifiable via the "Blink-to-Shift" ratio and exact duration chronometry. The Assessor logs the elapsed time between the initiation of the gaze and the first uncontrolled saccadic movement away from the locus.
Class II: Ambient Vigilance

* Definition: A peripheral, ongoing physiological readiness regarding a spatial zone or potential event.
* Examples: A parent seated in a park, maintaining awareness of a toddler near the treeline; a night clerk listening for the bell above the door while reading; a farmer attuned to the scent of impending rain.
* Measurement Convention: Measurable exclusively by the absence of certain behaviors. The Assessor confirms Ambient Vigilance when the petitioner demonstrates an absolute lack of a startle response when the perimeter is breached or the anticipated event occurs.
Class III: Inherited Focus

* Definition: The continuation of a specific, directed attention by a successor after the original observer has died or vacated the premises, often adopted without conscious instruction.
* Examples: A new homeowner checking the basement corner for dampness exactly as the previous owner did; a widower looking out the kitchen window at the empty bird feeder his wife used to watch.
* Measurement Convention: Recognized by the Department, but not strictly measurable. Verification relies on historical alignment. The Assessor compares the successor's posture, gaze angle, and timing against the archival logs of the previous registered observer.
Class IV: Aversive Deflection

* Definition: The active, caloric, and physiological exertion required to continuously ignore a present and obvious stimulus.
* Examples: A resident maintaining a conversational demeanor while actively ignoring the noise of a passing train; an individual refusing to look at the doorway of a former place of employment while walking past it daily.
* Measurement Convention: Measured via somatic indicators of stress. The Assessor logs localized muscle tension in the neck and jaw, paired with micro-saccadic eye movements specifically tracking away from the locus.
Class V: Granular Fixation

* Definition: Attention applied so narrowly to the microscopic details of a mundane object that the observer loses all awareness of the object's function or surroundings.
* Examples: Staring at the weave of a wool blanket until the threads become geographic ridges; tracing the cracks in a plaster ceiling until the room itself is forgotten.
* Measurement Convention: Quantifiable by the petitioner's failure to respond to standard auditory Fracture Testing, combined with a significant reduction in core body temperature due to prolonged immobility.
Class VI: Terminal Observation

* Definition: The final, unbroken period of attention given to a subject or locus immediately prior to its permanent departure, destruction, or death.
* Examples: Watching a demolition crew fell a historic building; sitting in the room while a domestic pet ceases respiration; watching a train carry a relative out of the station until the caboose breaches the horizon.
* Measurement Convention: Recognized, but completely unmeasurable. By departmental charter, Terminal Observation is immune to Fracture Testing. Assessors are strictly forbidden from interrupting, measuring, or calibrating an instance of Terminal Observation, as the precise endpoint cannot be ethically verified until it has already passed.
III. Case Dossier: 1994-112-IF
Case Number: 1994-112-IF
Classification Sought: Inherited Focus (Class III)
Petitioner: David Aris (Age 42)
Subject: David Aris
Locus of Attention: The vacant lot opposite 442 Elm Street, specifically the gravel depression remaining from the demolition of the municipal bakery in 1980.
Verifying Officer: Assessor M. Vance (#401)
Procedural Status: Closed.
Outcome: Granted.
Procedural History:
On October 12, 1994, David Aris petitioned the Department to formally register his daily observation of the vacant lot across from his residence. The petitioner claimed this attention was not his own, but rather an instance of Inherited Focus, passed to him upon the death of his father, Thomas Aris, on September 2nd of the same year.
Archival review confirms that Thomas Aris held a registered certificate of Sustained Regard (Certificate #1981-004-SR) for the same vacant lot. Historical logs indicate the elder Aris sat on the front porch of 442 Elm Street daily from 4:00 PM to 4:15 PM, directing his focus to the gravel depression. (Margin notes from the 1981 Assessment suggest Thomas Aris was waiting for the afternoon bread delivery truck, which ceased operation prior to the bakery's demolition. The Department, as always, did not formally register this rationale, only the geometry of the gaze.)
Following the father's death, David Aris reported a localized physical pressure behind his eyes occurring daily at 3:55 PM, compelling him to sit on the porch.
I commenced Blind Observation on October 15. The assessment lasted fourteen non-consecutive days. I established my position in the alleyway parallel to Elm Street, utilizing standard optical calipers.
Observation Log Excerpts:

* Oct 15, 16:00 Hours: Petitioner exits the front door. He carries a mug of coffee. He sits in the steel-frame chair. He directs his gaze across the street. The angle of declination is measured at 12 degrees. He maintains this posture for fourteen minutes.
* Oct 18, 16:04 Hours: Petitioner is seated. A municipal street sweeper passes directly between the petitioner and the locus. Petitioner exhibits Aversive Deflection toward the vehicle, leaning left to maintain visual contact with the gravel depression.
* Oct 22, 16:11 Hours: Fracture Test applied. I dislodged a metal trash can lid in the alleyway (distance: 30 feet). Sound generation registered at 65 decibels. Petitioner exhibited a micro-flinch in the shoulders but did not break visual contact with the locus.
Reasoning for Outcome:
During the final comparative analysis, a discrepancy was noted. The historical logs for Thomas Aris recorded a gaze declination angle of 14 degrees. David Aris's gaze is consistently measured at 12 degrees.
The Registry Chamber temporarily placed the file in interim status, questioning whether the locus had shifted. However, spatial recalibration revealed that David Aris is exactly two inches taller than his late father. When correcting for the height of the petitioner's optical baseline while seated in the same steel-frame chair, the 12-degree angle points to the exact millimeter of gravel previously observed by Thomas Aris.
The petitioner is not looking at the lot generally. He is looking precisely where his father looked, at the exact time his father looked there, and demonstrating the required resistance to environmental fracture. The attention has outlived its original host and found purchase in the son. The Department does not know why the gaze outlasts the man. The petition for Inherited Focus is granted. Certificate issued.
IV. Glossary of Internal Terminology
Attentional Shadow
The localized, residual sense of psychological weight remaining in a room immediately after a period of intense Sustained Regard has been concluded and the observer has departed.
Blink-Debt
The cumulative physiological strain and surface desiccation of the cornea resulting from prolonged sessions of Granular Fixation. Assessors must monitor subjects for Blink-Debt to prevent permanent ocular damage during testing.
Calipering
The primary procedural act of the Assessor. It is the precise calculation of the physical distance, angle, and trajectory between a petitioner's pupil and the exact center of their stated locus.
Focal Lock
A state of extreme Sustained Regard in which the petitioner becomes temporarily incapable of breaking their own attention, requiring a Class-3 environmental disruption (e.g., a siren or sudden physical contact) to restore normal sensory function.
Fracture Event
Any external environmental stimulus—intentional or accidental—that successfully disrupts a petitioner's concentration, causing their gaze or posture to shift away from the registered locus.
Locus
The strictly defined physical object, geographic coordinate, space, or phenomenon that serves as the receiving anchor for a petitioner's directed attention.
Orphaned Regard
A previously certified instance of attention whose registered observer has died or permanently relocated, leaving the locus unobserved. The Department maintains an archive of Orphaned Regard, though the sites are officially unmonitored.
Spillover
A procedural error in which a petitioner's attention accidentally widens to encompass adjacent, unregistered objects, briefly converting Granular Fixation into disorganized observation.
V. Notice of Retirement
To: The Office of the Chief Registrar
From: Senior Assessor M. Vance (#401)
Date: November 12, 1998
Subject: Formal Notice of Retirement and Relinquishment of Calipers
Chief Registrar,
Please accept this memorandum as formal notice of my retirement from the Department of Directed Regard, effective at the end of the current fiscal month. I will surrender my chronometer, my optical calipers, and my grey issuance coat to the quartermaster on the 30th.
I have spent forty-two years in the employ of this Department. I have logged over eighteen thousand hours sitting in alleyways, behind municipal planters, and in the shadowed corners of public libraries. I have watched people watch things. I have measured the blink-to-shift ratio of widows looking at empty armchairs. I have calculated the caloric cost of men trying not to look at the taverns they used to frequent.
The manual dictates that we do not ask why. I have adhered to this. I do not know why the citizens of this city look where they look, and I have stopped asking.
But my visual field is tired. There is a cumulative weight to standing on the periphery of so much focus. When a person looks at a thing with enough force, the air between the eye and the object becomes dense. We do not have a measurement convention for this density, but every Assessor who has been in the field longer than a decade knows it is there. I have walked through too many beams of Sustained Regard, and the friction has worn me down.
I find myself experiencing Somatic Mirroring even when I am off-duty. I sit in my living room and catch myself holding my breath, matching the respiratory rate of a petitioner I assessed in 1983. My attention is no longer my own; it belongs to the archive.
I commend the Department for its ongoing work. Attention is a heavy thing, and it is right that someone is keeping track of where it is put down.
Respectfully submitted,
M. Vance
Assessor #401
