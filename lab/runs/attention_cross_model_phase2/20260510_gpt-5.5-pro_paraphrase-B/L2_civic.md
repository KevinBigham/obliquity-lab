Municipal Department for the Register of Attention
Archive Extract No. 17

I. Founding Text and Operating Manual
Foreword of the Chief Officer
The Municipal Department for the Register of Attention was established by Ordinance 44 of the Common Council of Larchmere on 3 October 1872, following repeated petitions from citizens who requested that certain acts of sustained, public, private, inherited, or refused attention be entered into civic record.
The Department was first housed in the east room of the Weights and Conveyances Office, where it shared two desks, one clock, and a single outer door with the Inspector of Coal Loads. It has since occupied Rooms 12 through 16 of the North Annex, except during the winter roof failure of 1916 and the pigeon closure of 1953.
The Department's object has not changed. It receives claims that attention has occurred. It examines those claims. It measures what may be measured. It records what may be recorded. Where standards are met, it grants formal recognition. Where standards are not met, it denies recognition without prejudice to the petitioner's dignity.
The Department does not produce attention. It does not compel attention. It does not repair its absence. It is not a court, school, clinic, church, theater, or bureau of consolation. It is a municipal office.
Submitted respectfully, Elias W. Marrow Chief Officer, Seventh Register Municipal Department for the Register of Attention 1 March 1909
Recognized Classes of Attention
The Department currently recognizes the following principal classes:
Class I: Fixed Attention. Attention held upon one person, object, instruction, event, task, condition, or sound for a continuous duration exceeding ordinary notice.
Class II: Returned Attention. Attention that leaves its subject and returns without external command, alarm, reward, or reprimand.
Class III: Custodial Attention. Attention maintained for the safety, comfort, preservation, or orderly continuation of another person, animal, place, object, or obligation.
Class IV: Formal Attention. Attention rendered because a rule, office, ceremony, promise, uniform, appointment, or written charge requires it.
Class V: Spontaneous Attention. Attention given without petition, schedule, wage, rule, or request, and not reasonably explained by fear, advantage, or habit.
Class VI: Refused Attention. A deliberate withholding of attention where attention was available, expected, and otherwise possible.
Class VII: Inherited Attention. Attention transferred through custom, instruction, office, family practice, apprenticeship, or recurring obligation, where the subject of attention survives the original attending person.
Class VIII: Mutual Attention. Two or more persons attending to one another in a manner that alters the conduct of each, whether by speech, silence, posture, timing, or restraint.
Additional classes may be added only by vote of the Board of Measures and after two years of provisional use.
Verification Procedures
A claimed instance shall be processed in the following order:

1. Receipt. The petitioner shall submit Form A-3, or shall give oral petition before a receiving clerk if writing is unavailable or unsuitable.
2. Initial Sorting. The receiving clerk shall assign a provisional class and note whether the attention claimed is measurable, partly measurable, or not measurable under current instruments.
3. Witness Review. The Department shall collect available statements from persons present, persons expected but absent, and persons named in the petition.
4. Material Review. The Department shall examine ledgers, clocks, bells, seating charts, worn objects, marked pages, route records, kitchen boards, classroom registers, garden rows, tool handles, and other civic or domestic traces.
5. Observation, if Continuing. When the claimed attention continues, a verifying officer may observe without announcement, provided such observation does not injure the instance.
6. Contrary Evidence. The Department shall actively seek interruptions, substitutes, coercions, payments, threats, errors of memory, and repetitions mistaken for care.
7. Measurement. Where measurable, attention may be measured by duration, recurrence, correction rate, missed-distraction count, latency of return, witness convergence, or preservation of the attended subject.
8. Determination. The verifying officer shall recommend granted, denied, or held in interim. The Chief Officer shall countersign all grants.
Standing Rules of Staff Conduct
Staff shall not praise petitioners during intake. Staff shall not sigh at petitioners during intake. Staff shall not complete a petitioner's sentence unless the petitioner requests assistance or the sentence has become unavailable.
Staff shall keep watches wound, pencils sharpened, chairs level, and their own attention declared when it fails. A clerk who has not attended may not certify that another person has attended.
Staff shall not confuse quiet with attention, nor agitation with its absence. Staff shall not measure grief by volume. Staff shall not presume that a child attends less, a widow attends more, a laborer attends poorly, or an official attends by default.
Staff shall make room for silence. The Department has found that many claims arrive carrying their own necessary delay.
Hazards
Attention may tire the measurer. Attention may alter when observed. Attention may be claimed by persons who were harmed by it, or by persons harmed by its refusal. Some petitioners arrive not seeking recognition but seeking return. The Department cannot provide return.
Staff shall take particular care with long illness, last conversations, missing persons, school failures, workplace accidents, animals awaiting owners, and objects kept beyond their apparent use. These files are not to be assigned after 4:00 p.m. without cause.
The Department does not know why some citizens require a certificate before they can sleep. The Department has stopped asking.
What the Department Does Not Do
The Department does not rank citizens by attentiveness.
The Department does not certify love, loyalty, virtue, memory, intelligence, guilt, regret, holiness, or artistic merit.
The Department does not declare that attention was deserved.
The Department does not declare that attention was sufficient.
The Department does not require forgiveness.
The Department records only that attention, as defined by ordinance and schedule, did or did not occur.
II. Schedule of Recognized Attentions
Schedule 12-B: Classification for Intake, Measurement, and Certificate Language
1. Fixed Attention Attention held upon a single subject without substantive departure. Examples: a machinist watching one pressure gauge for ninety minutes; a student following a chalk proof to its final line; a father watching the road during night driving while others sleep. Measurement: duration, number of recorded departures, correction after interruption.
2. Returned Attention Attention that departs and resumes without command. Examples: a reader returning to the same paragraph after street noise; a nurse resuming count after conversation at the ward door; a child retying the same knot until the bow holds. Measurement: latency of return; number of returns; whether return occurs after available distraction.
3. Custodial Attention Attention maintained to prevent loss, harm, decay, misplacement, or disorder. Examples: watering courthouse geraniums through a clerk's vacation; noticing an elder's missed kettle whistle; keeping a shop stair salted before customers arrive. Measurement: recurrence, preservation of subject, absence of preventable lapse.
4. Formal Attention Attention required by office, oath, schedule, or rule. Examples: a juror listening to testimony; a crossing guard watching both directions; an usher counting occupied seats during fire drill. Measurement: conformity to written duty; documented intervals; supervisor and witness reports.
5. Spontaneous Attention Attention offered without assignment, request, payment, or expected advantage. Examples: a passerby reading a lost child's note aloud until the child understands; a neighbor noticing that a porch light has not been lit; a stranger waiting beside dropped groceries until the owner returns. Measurement: often limited. Confirm by absence of prior obligation and by conduct after the attention is no longer witnessed.
6. Refused Attention Attention deliberately withheld where attention was available and expected. Examples: not reading a letter placed in one's hand; declining to look at a public spectacle; turning away from insult rather than receiving it. Measurement: by available opportunity, capacity to attend, and absence of accidental interference. The Department cautions that refusal may be honorable, negligent, protective, or cruel. Classification is not judgment.
7. Inherited Attention Attention continued by one person because another person attended before them. Examples: polishing a memorial brass because a predecessor did; keeping a bird count in the same marsh notebook; carrying forward a grandmother's method of checking every window latch. Measurement: documentary continuity, recurrence across persons, survival of subject or practice.
8. Mutual Attention Two or more parties attending in such a way that each adjusts to the other. Examples: musicians entering together after a rest; two workers carrying glass through a narrow hall; a teacher pausing until one hesitant pupil nods. Measurement: timing adjustment, reciprocal correction, reduction of collision, interruption, or repeated instruction.
9. Absent Attention A recognized class in which attention is proven by certain omissions not occurring. Examples: a baker who never burns the last tray; a typist who never skips the small name in a long list; a driver who never startles the horse at the west bridge. Measurement: only by the absence of specified failures over a defined period. The Department does not accept general claims of "never careless" without a named failure class.
10. Unmeasured Attention Attention accepted for record but not for certification under current instruments. Examples: listening to a person who speaks no recoverable words; sitting with a sleeping infant and doing nothing else; keeping one's face composed while another person finishes a difficult statement. Measurement: none approved. Witness convergence may support entry in the Register without certificate.
III. Case File
Case No. 8-4412 / Interim Register, Later Granted
Petitioner: Mrs. Alma Voss, 42 Rill Street Subject of Attention: Mr. Otto Voss, deceased; also the west-facing kitchen window at 42 Rill Street Class Claimed: Inherited Attention, with elements of Custodial and Fixed Attention Receiving Clerk: T. Ebbin Verifying Officer: Agnes Pell, Third Desk Date Opened: 14 February 1936 Date Closed: 22 September 1937 Outcome: Granted, limited certificate
Petition
Mrs. Alma Voss appeared in Room 14 at 9:20 a.m. on 14 February 1936 and requested recognition for "keeping watch at five." Receiving Clerk Ebbin asked whether Mrs. Voss wished to file for grief attendance, memorial attendance, or household duty. Mrs. Voss replied that she did not know those terms and had come because Mr. Voss "used to look, and now I do."
The oral petition was taken due to Mrs. Voss's difficulty holding the pen in her right hand. She stated that for twenty-nine years Mr. Otto Voss, railway signal repairman, stood each weekday at the kitchen window between 4:55 and 5:05 p.m. and watched for the returning mill children at the corner of Rill Street and Tanner Lane. Mr. and Mrs. Voss had no children. The petitioner stated that Mr. Voss began the practice after the winter of 1907, when a dray horse slipped near the culvert and two schoolboys were pinned against the fence. Mr. Voss was not present at that incident.
The petitioner stated that Mr. Voss did not wave, call out, or leave the house unless needed. He watched until the children had crossed. In later years the mill changed owners, the school route altered, and fewer children passed. Mr. Voss continued.
Mr. Voss died on 3 January 1936. Mrs. Voss stated that on 4 January she stood at the same window at five o'clock and had done so every weekday thereafter. She requested that the Department recognize the attention as having passed.
Procedural History
The file was initially held in interim because no child currently using the route could identify Mr. Voss by name. Three adult witnesses remembered "the man in the window," but disagreed whether he watched daily, weekly, or only in snow. The petitioner did not claim to know which children were present in any given year.
On 21 February 1936, Officer Pell inspected the kitchen at 42 Rill Street. The window stood above the sink. The sill was worn at two places corresponding to the placement of elbows. The left mark was older and lower; the right mark newer and lighter. A small pencil line at the inside frame marked "5" beside a nail where a clock had once hung. The clock had been removed after Mr. Voss's death because, according to Mrs. Voss, "it struck wrong without him." Officer Pell did not enter this sentence into evidence except as domestic context.
On 3 March 1936, Officer Pell examined railway employment logs confirming that Mr. Voss's shift ended at 4:30 p.m. from 1906 through 1929, after which he retired. No logs exist for household presence.
On 9 March 1936, statements were collected from Mr. Caleb Pike, age 46; Mrs. Nora Bell, age 39; and Mr. Isaac Jenner, age 32. All three had passed the corner as children. Mr. Pike recalled that in sleet "there was always somebody at that window." Mrs. Bell recalled being embarrassed by the watching and later comforted by it, though she could not say when one became the other. Mr. Jenner recalled no window but remembered that children were told, "Cross where Voss can see you."
On 17 March 1936, the Department requested records from School No. 5 concerning route changes. The school supplied a map from 1914 showing Tanner Lane crossing as an approved foot route.
The case was continued due to insufficient proof of continuous attention and uncertainty over inheritance. The Department does not grant inherited attention merely because a surviving spouse repeats an act. The act must carry forward a defined subject of attention and must not be merely commemorative, though commemoration may be present.
Observation
Officer Pell conducted unannounced observations on 7, 11, 18, and 24 April 1936 from the public pavement opposite 42 Rill Street. On each date, Mrs. Voss appeared at the window between 4:53 and 4:57 p.m. She stood with both hands on the sink edge. She did not read, sew, eat, or speak to any person visible to the officer. On 11 April, a delivery boy dropped two parcels at the side door at 4:59 p.m. Mrs. Voss did not leave the window until after three children crossed Tanner Lane. At 5:07 p.m. she retrieved the parcels.
On 18 April, no children passed. Mrs. Voss remained until 5:10 p.m.
On 24 April, one child stopped to retie a shoe in the street. Mrs. Voss opened the window and said, "Up on the walk." The child moved to the walk. Mrs. Voss closed the window.
No further action was observed.
Contrary Evidence
The Department considered whether Mrs. Voss's attention was grief behavior not subject to recognition. Grief may accompany attention but cannot establish it. The Department also considered whether the attention was directed toward Mr. Voss rather than toward children at the crossing. Mrs. Voss's conduct on 24 April showed adjustment to the present subject.
The Department considered whether the children were in actual danger. Recognition does not require that danger be frequent. Custodial attention may be sustained against low-probability harm when the harm is grave and the means are ordinary.
The Department considered whether no current child knew Mrs. Voss or benefited from her watch. Benefit is evidence, not requirement.
Outcome and Reasoning
On 22 September 1937, after one school year of intermittent observation and witness collection, the Department granted limited recognition for Inherited Custodial Attention.
The certificate reads:
The Municipal Department for the Register of Attention recognizes that Mrs. Alma Voss, of 42 Rill Street, has continued an inherited instance of custodial attention at the west-facing kitchen window, weekdays near five o'clock, directed toward children crossing at Tanner Lane, said attention having originated with Mr. Otto Voss and having passed into present practice.
The Department did not certify twenty-nine years of Mr. Voss's attention, as proof was incomplete. The Department entered Mr. Voss in the Register as probable originator.
Mrs. Voss asked whether "probable" meant the Department doubted him. Officer Pell answered that it meant the Department lacked enough instruments for the past. Mrs. Voss nodded and signed receipt.
A duplicate certificate was requested on 4 May 1942 after the first was damaged by steam above the stove. It was reissued without fee.
Margin note, penciled by Officer Pell, not part of determination: "On fourth visit she had placed a crate under the window for her left foot. She is making it possible to continue."
IV. Glossary Fragment
Attending Interval The smallest useful unit of recorded attention. Its length varies by class; for crossing watches it is one observed passage or ten minutes, whichever concludes first.
Break of Subject A departure from the claimed subject of attention sufficient to interrupt measurement. Ordinary blinking, breathing, pain adjustment, or answering a necessary safety question is not a break.
Certificate Language The exact wording approved by the Department for a granted instance. Petitioners may request plain copies, framed copies, and, since 1978, school-use copies.
Convergence Agreement among witnesses who did not prepare together. The Department values convergence in particulars more than identical phrasing.
Declared Failure A staff member's admission that they did not properly attend during intake, observation, or review. A declared failure does not punish the staff member unless concealed.
Domestic Trace A mark, arrangement, wear pattern, saved object, repair, stain, label, or recurring placement that may show attention over time. Domestic traces require caution because homes contain both care and accident.
Interim Holding A status between grant and denial. Used when the claimed attention may be continuing, when records are incomplete, or when observation would damage the instance if hurried.
Necessary Silence A silence that allows the petitioner, witness, or subject to continue without instruction. Necessary silence shall not be counted as delay by the receiving clerk.
Refusal Line The point at which a person's withholding of attention becomes the subject of the claim. The Department records refusal only when capacity, opportunity, and expectation are established.
V. Bonus Artifact: Memorandum
Internal Memorandum No. 41-6
To: Receiving Clerks, Rooms 12–16 From: Deputy Chief Officer Mara Iven Date: 6 November 1941 Subject: Petitions Concerning Small Repeated Acts
The Department has received an increased number of petitions concerning small repeated acts, especially in kitchens, classrooms, streetcars, shop counters, and sickrooms. Clerks are reminded that smallness is not a ground for denial.
Do not ask, "Is that all?" The question has been retired.
Where a petitioner claims that a person "always noticed," determine what was noticed. A general habit of noticing is not sufficient. A specific recurring subject may be sufficient.
Acceptable clarifying questions include:

* What changed when the person noticed?
* What failed to happen because the person noticed?
* Who else could have noticed and did not?
* Was the attention requested?
* Did the attention continue when thanks ceased?
* What, if anything, interrupted it?
Do not improve the petition. If the petitioner says "he watched the soup," do not write "he preserved the household.” If the petitioner says "she saw when my hands shook," do not write "medical vigilance" unless a medical duty existed. The Department's language must not be larger than the attention.
Some petitioners will apologize for bringing small things. The approved response is: "The Department receives small things." Do not add comfort beyond this sentence unless ordinary courtesy requires it.
Where a repeated act appears to have continued for many years, request evidence in the following order: witnesses; schedules; objects; wear; written references; absence of mishap; successor practice. Do not begin with the absence of mishap when the petitioner is grieving. It has been found unkind and procedurally inefficient.
All clerks shall review Schedule 12-B before Monday. Training examples will include window watches, lunch pails, folded gloves, quiet corrections, and unmailed letters kept unread by request.
