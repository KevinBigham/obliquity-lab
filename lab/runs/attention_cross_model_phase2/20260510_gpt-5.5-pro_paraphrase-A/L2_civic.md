I. Founding Document and Operating Handbook
Director's Foreword to the Third Consolidated Handbook, 1911
The Office of Attention, Measure, and Acknowledgement was established in 1878 for the receipt, examination, recording, gauging, and, where appropriate, formal acknowledgement of instances in which one human person has given attention to another person, object, charge, absence, injury, duty, promise, place, animal, household, route, or time.
The Office was founded after the winter inquiries of 1876 and 1877, in which the Council received numerous petitions concerning acts of attention that had no legal value, no wage value, and no clear place among the existing records of charity, inheritance, employment, guardianship, or public service. These acts included vigils, return visits, careful watching, repeated notice, attendance at thresholds, the maintenance of small articles belonging to the dead, and the keeping of time for persons who could not keep it for themselves.
The Council determined that not every civic fact is an injury, debt, birth, death, conveyance, license, or crime. Some facts consist in the sustained direction of a person toward what may otherwise have passed without registration. Such facts are not thereby large. They are not thereby sacred. They are, however, facts.
This Office does not confer moral rank. It does not reward. It does not settle family disputes except as to the record before it. It does not improve the dead, amend a marriage, repair a household, cure loneliness, or determine whether love was present. The Office records attention.
For purposes of the Office, attention is not a declaration of feeling. It is the conduct of noticing, returning to, holding in view, distinguishing, guarding, repeating, or refraining, where such conduct can be shown by testimony, instrument, schedule, mark, wear, absence, or other admissible indication.
The categories presently acknowledged are as follows:

1. Direct Regard: attention given to a person, animal, object, or place by sustained looking, listening, reading, naming, or tactile examination.
2. Custodial Attention: attention expressed through care of a charge, including feeding, airing, cleaning, covering, counting, accompanying, or preventing loss.
3. Vigilant Attention: attention maintained during risk, illness, waiting, danger, public alarm, or uncertain return.
4. Recurrent Attention: attention proved by repeated visits, entries, checks, calls, substitutions, or marks over time.
5. Ceremonial Attention: attention given by public or private observance according to a rule, date, place, vow, anniversary, or inherited custom.
6. Hereditary Attention: attention transmitted within a household, office, trade, parish, school, or other continuing body, where the present act depends upon an earlier obligation to notice.
7. Disputed Attention: attention claimed by one party and denied, minimized, or reassigned by another.
8. Negative or Abstentive Attention: attention shown by not doing what would ordinarily be done, including not touching, not entering, not speaking, not replacing, not correcting, or not claiming.
9. Terminal Attention: attention directed toward a closing, ending, death, last use, last sound, last meal, final visit, final lesson, or final public service.
10. Unmeasured Attention: attention admitted to the Register but not gauge-able under present methods.
Verification Procedure
When an instance is asserted, the receiving clerk shall first separate the petition from any praise, complaint, accusation, doctrine, quarrel, or request for remedy not before the Office. The clerk shall assign a provisional number and notify the petitioner that assertion is not registration.
A verifying officer shall then gather, as applicable: sworn statements; household records; letters; diaries; route books; watchman's slips; school ledgers; repair tickets; weather observations; hospital notes; burial schedules; kitchen lists; object inventories; worn surfaces; unopened packages; repeated dates; regular absences; and the testimony of persons neither favored nor harmed by acknowledgement.
The officer shall distinguish duration from intensity, return from intention, silence from neglect, and habit from charge. Where possible, the officer shall conduct a still hour, during which the relevant object, place, or record is examined without petitioner commentary. No officer shall infer attention from tears alone, nor deny it because tears were absent.
The finding shall be one of the following: Acknowledged and Gauged, Acknowledged Without Gauge, Declined, Held Pending, or Removed from Jurisdiction. A concise reason shall be entered.
Standing Rules of Personnel Conduct
Personnel shall not console petitioners in the name of the Office. Private kindness is not prohibited, but it shall not be confused with an act of record.
Personnel shall not laugh at petitions, even after hours. Many petitions appear small because the person who would have explained them is unavailable.
Personnel shall not accept gifts of flowers, coins, bottles, food, gloves, photographs, or keys. Keys may be received only as evidence.
Personnel shall not complete an instance on behalf of a petitioner. An officer may observe a chair placed by a bed but may not place it.
Personnel shall not remark that a petitioner "must have loved" or "cannot have loved" another person. The Office has no instrument for that statement.
Personnel shall keep their eyes rested. Officers assigned to Vigilant Attention shall rotate after six hours. Officers assigned to Terminal Attention shall not be assigned to school matters the following morning without consent.
Hazards
The principal hazards are over-identification, fatigue of regard, false enlargement, household capture, commemorative contagion, and the improper transfer of official attention to private persons.
Some petitioners attempt to make the Office see what they have seen, as if duplication were justice. The Office may acknowledge a burden without taking it up.
Objects long kept by petitioners may produce in officers an excessive tenderness. The officer shall note condition, placement, use, and continuity. The officer shall not cradle, stroke, kiss, repair, or apologize to an object under examination.
Repeated reading of last letters is discouraged. One reading shall suffice unless a material question remains.
What the Office Does Not Undertake
The Office does not determine affection, virtue, guilt, betrayal, beauty, sanity, worthiness, or the rightness of a life.
The Office does not certify that the attended thing was important. It certifies, where proven, that attention was given.
The Office does not compel gratitude. It does not notify the dead. It does not restore missed occasions. It does not instruct citizens how to attend.
The Office records. In some cases, this is enough for the city. In some cases, it is not enough for the petitioner. The Office has stopped asking that the two be the same.
II. Schedule of Recognized Attentions
Schedule A: Official Sorting, Revised 1934; Reaffirmed 1962, 1988, 2019
A-1. Direct Regard A person's sustained sensory attention to a distinct subject. Examples: a nurse listening for a change in breathing; a child reading the same inscription each market day; a mechanic examining a crack before naming it harmless. Measurement: minutes of uninterrupted regard, excluding conversation not directed to the subject.
A-2. Custodial Attention Attention expressed through keeping a charge from spoilage, loss, exposure, confusion, or hunger. Examples: turning a bedridden man every three hours; watering courthouse plants during closure; checking a neighbor's stove after snow. Measurement: number of completed acts against a required schedule.
A-3. Vigilant Attention Attention held during uncertainty, risk, or expected arrival. Examples: watching a river gauge during flood season; waiting at a station for a delayed son; listening overnight for a fevered child. Measurement: span of wakeful attendance, adjusted for relief periods.
A-4. Recurrent Attention Attention proved less by duration than by return. Examples: visiting a grave on non-holiday Tuesdays; inspecting a loose stair each morning; copying weather from the same window for forty years. Measurement: count of returns, regularity index, and unexplained lapses.
A-5. Ceremonial Attention Attention performed according to rule, date, office, vow, or civic form. Examples: lowering a flag at the same hour; setting an empty plate on a birthday; reading names aloud before dismissal. Measurement: conformity to prescribed elements. Feeling is not measured.
A-6. Hereditary Attention Attention inherited by succession rather than invented by the present actor. Examples: the third baker in a family leaving bread at the almshouse door; a school custodian preserving a retired teacher's bell; a clerk maintaining a list begun by a predecessor. Measurement: chain length, break history, and evidence of knowing receipt.
A-7. Disputed Attention Attention asserted in the presence of contrary claims. Examples: two daughters claiming the same hospital vigils; an apprentice asserting care of tools denied by the master; a town disputing who kept watch at a closed mine. Measurement: not made until dispute is resolved or bounded. May be acknowledged in portions.
A-8. Abstentive Attention Attention shown by deliberate non-action. Examples: not repainting a doorframe marked with children's heights; not moving a chair after a death; not correcting a name a dying person had chosen to misremember. Measurement: gauge-able only by absence of ordinary conduct and evidence that the absence was maintained knowingly.
A-9. Terminal Attention Attention given to finality. Examples: staying through the last shift of a factory; recording a final sermon's length; feeding an animal on the morning of its killing. Measurement: proximity to the ending, completeness of attendance, and whether the attending person left before closure.
A-10. Civic Attention Attention rendered to the public without private possession. Examples: a crossing guard counting children; a ranger noting visitor tracks after a storm; a clerk reading every complaint before filing. Measurement: number of persons or matters attended, error rate, and evidence of distinction between cases.
A-11. Borrowed Attention Attention undertaken because another person could not perform it. Examples: reading a letter aloud to an illiterate mother; standing in a wedding photograph for an absent brother; taking notes for a sleeping watchman. Measurement: admissible only when the original charge is shown.
A-12. Unmeasured Attention Attention acknowledged but not gauge-able by the Office. Examples: a person noticing a change in another person's manner before any act followed; a long quiet companionship where no schedule, object, or witness remains; the attention of the dying to those who believe themselves to be attending. Measurement: none. Entry by Director's concurrence only.
III. Case File No. 77-14-603
Petition of Mrs. Elianor Voss Concerning the Kitchen Window of 12 Rowen Street
Classification Requested: Recurrent Attention; Abstentive Attention; possible Hereditary Attention Petitioner: Mrs. Elianor Voss, widow, aged 71 Subject of Attention: The kitchen window, rear elevation, 12 Rowen Street Person Alleged to Have Attended: Mr. August Voss, deceased, husband of petitioner Verifying Officer: Assistant Registrar Mara Pell Date Petition Received: 3 February 1977 Date of Determination: 21 April 1977 Outcome: Acknowledged Without Gauge as Recurrent Attention; Acknowledged and Gauged in limited part as Abstentive Attention; Hereditary Attention declined
Statement of Petition
Mrs. Voss appeared in person and requested that the Office record "what he did with the window." Upon questioning, she stated that her late husband, Mr. August Voss, had for thirty-two years looked from the kitchen window at approximately 6:10 each morning, except Sundays, to confirm whether the light had appeared in the upstairs room of the house opposite.
The house opposite, 13 Rowen Street, was occupied from 1943 to 1972 by Mr. Ludo Arendt, a widower employed at the municipal foundry, and his daughter, Miss Lene Arendt, later Mrs. Hale. Mrs. Voss stated that Miss Arendt, as a girl, was required to leave for school before her father woke. The father had once overslept during a winter gas leak, after which Mr. Voss began looking for the girl's lamp. If the lamp did not appear, Mr. Voss would cross the street and knock.
Mrs. Voss further stated that after Miss Arendt married and left the city, Mr. Voss continued to look at the same upstairs room. "There was no girl there," Mrs. Voss said. "He knew that. He looked because he had become the person who looked."
Mrs. Voss sought acknowledgement because the house at 12 Rowen Street was to be sold, and the buyer had stated an intention to enlarge the kitchen window, removing the worn place on the sill where Mr. Voss's left hand had rested.
Procedural History
Initial intake found the petition admissible under Recurrent Attention, with possible Custodial Attention during the years 1944 to 1956, when Miss Arendt was a minor. Custodial Attention was later withdrawn by the petitioner, who stated, "He did not raise her. He only noticed whether she had risen."
The Office requested testimony from Mrs. Hale, formerly Miss Lene Arendt. Mrs. Hale replied by letter dated 18 February 1977. She confirmed that as a child she lit a lamp before leaving for school and that on "three or perhaps four" mornings Mr. Voss crossed the street to wake the household. She remembered one occasion in which the stove had gone out and another in which her father had taken ill. She did not know that Mr. Voss continued the practice after her marriage. She wrote, "I am sorry to learn it, though I do not know why I am sorry."
A search of municipal weather records confirmed severe morning darkness during winter months but did not materially assist.
The buyer of 12 Rowen Street, Mr. Peter Clave, submitted a statement through counsel. He did not oppose acknowledgement but requested that the Office make clear that any entry would not restrict alteration of the property. The Office so notified all parties. The Office does not impose preservation.
On 9 March 1977, Assistant Registrar Pell conducted a site inspection. The kitchen window faced east-northeast toward the rear upper room of 13 Rowen Street at a distance of 31 feet 4 inches. The sight line was unobstructed. The sill at 12 Rowen Street showed a flattened and darkened oval area on the left interior board, 3.2 inches by 1.1 inches. The wear was consistent with repeated placement of a hand in the same position. There were no initials, marks, or inscriptions.
Mrs. Voss produced Mr. Voss's work cards from 1945 through 1968, showing a foundry shift commencing at 7:00. She also produced a kitchen clock stopped at 6:10 on the morning of Mr. Voss's death. The clock was not admitted as timing evidence. Mrs. Voss stated she had stopped it herself.
The Office interviewed Mr. Pellam, retired milkman, who delivered on Rowen Street from 1951 to 1963. He recalled seeing Mr. Voss at the window "more mornings than not," holding a cup in his right hand. He did not know what Mr. Voss watched.
Mrs. Voss was asked whether Mr. Voss ever spoke of the practice. She answered that he did not, except once in 1961, when she asked why he still looked after the girl had gone. He replied, "The room is still there."
Findings
The Office finds sufficient evidence that Mr. Voss engaged in Recurrent Attention toward the upstairs room of 13 Rowen Street from approximately 1944 until his death in 1976.
The Office declines to gauge the full recurrence. Although the period alleged is long, the exact number of mornings cannot be reliably established. The petitioner's memory is sincere but not countable. Work records show opportunity, not performance. The milkman's statement supports recurrence but not frequency. The worn sill supports repetition but not schedule.
The Office further finds a limited instance of Abstentive Attention from 1972 to 1976. During these years Mr. Voss knew the original reason for looking had ceased. He did not replace the act with another morning task. He did not cease because its practical use had ended. The attention was maintained by abstention from the ordinary correction of habit. This portion is gauge-able by duration: four years, three months, approximate.
The claim of Hereditary Attention is declined. No successor received or continued the practice. Mrs. Voss stated she did not look after Mr. Voss's death. Mrs. Hale did not know of the continuance. The buyer is not bound and has not accepted the charge. A practice may be long without being inherited.
The Office notes that the attended room itself did not request, receive, or require acknowledgement. The acknowledgement concerns Mr. Voss's conduct only.
Determination
Entered: Acknowledged Without Gauge, Recurrent Attention, Mr. August Voss, kitchen window of 12 Rowen Street toward rear upper room of 13 Rowen Street, approximate years 1944–1976.
Entered: Acknowledged and Gauged, Abstentive Attention, same subject, approximate years 1972–1976, after known cessation of original practical occasion.
Declined: Hereditary Attention.
No preservation order issued. None available.
Margin Note, Pencil, Unsigned

IV. Glossary Fragment
Acknowledgement The formal entry of an instance into the Register. It does not mean approval, reward, or endorsement.
Gauge A number, range, schedule, or other measure attached to an acknowledged instance. A gauge may be exact, approximate, limited, or refused.
Still Hour A period of officer examination during which the petitioner may not explain, praise, defend, or narrate the subject. The purpose is to see what the evidence can bear without assistance.
Ordinary Correction The change a reasonable person would usually make when the practical reason for an act has ended. Not making that change may support Abstentive Attention.
Lapse A break in an attentive practice. A lapse may be excused, unexplained, material, ceremonial, weather-caused, illness-caused, or fatal to the claim.
Tender Evidence Evidence likely to move an officer but weak for the record, such as a folded handkerchief, a kept ribbon, or an object preserved in a drawer. Tender evidence is never discarded, but it is seldom sufficient alone.
Household Capture The condition in which an officer begins to accept a household's private vocabulary as official terminology. Personnel are trained to resist capture without insulting the household.
Attentive Burden An act of attention that began as care and became obligation after the first reason passed. The Office acknowledges such burdens when proved but does not decide whether they should have continued.
Refusal of Enlargement The rule that an officer shall not make an instance larger because the petitioner has suffered. Grief may explain the petition. It does not expand the evidence.
V. Bonus Artifact: Retirement Letter of Senior Clerk Tomas Ivern
Filed Internally, 30 June 1952
To the Director,
I submit my retirement from the Office after forty-one years, three months, and nine days of service, not including the two weeks in 1918 when the fever closed the south rooms and I copied entries from home.
I have no complaint against the Office. It has been a good place to work, by which I mean that the chairs were poor, the ink was reliable, the windows were washed in April, and the work did not pretend to be other than itself.
I was first assigned to Recurrent Attention. I believed then that repetition was the easiest kind to record. It had dates. It had marks. It had route slips. I later learned that repetition is where citizens hide the things they are least able to name. They say, "He came every Thursday." They mean several things, only one of which is Thursday.
I have made errors. In 1926, I declined the petition of a boy who claimed his mother listened at his bedroom door each night. I wrote that no independent evidence supported the assertion. I was correct by the handbook. I was not correct by the door, which I did not examine. The boy returned in 1939 with the door removed from the house. There was a worn place on the hall side at the height of an ear. The case was reopened and acknowledged. The boy, then a man, shook my hand and said the delay was understandable. It was not.
I ask that my desk blotter be destroyed rather than issued. It contains names.
I leave my magnifier to the Evidence Room, my sleeve guards to any clerk with thin wrists, and my copy of the 1911 Handbook to the new intake officer, provided she is told that the book is not old because it is wise. It is old because people keep bringing us the same kinds of things.
Respectfully,
Tomas Ivern Senior Clerk, Office of Attention, Measure, and Acknowledgement Badge 18
