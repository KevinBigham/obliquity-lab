THE BUREAU OF DIRECTED NOTICE
UNITED STATES DEPARTMENT OF THE INTERIOR
GENERAL OPERATIONS HANDBOOK, VOL. IX
ESTABLISHED 1892
DIRECTOR'S FOREWORD
The work of the Bureau of Directed Notice is the work of the ledger. Since 1892, this office has operated on a single, foundational mandate: human attention is a finite, verifiable resource. It is expended, it is withheld, and it leaves a permanent impression upon the subjects and objects toward which it is directed. It is the position of the Bureau that an hour of sustained human notice is a civic fact. It has weight. It occurs in time and space. When it goes unrecorded, a unit of human effort is lost to the administrative record.
We do not judge the worthiness of the object receiving the notice. We do not ask why a citizen has looked, listened, or maintained vigil. The Bureau leaves motivation to the clergy and the medical sciences. Our officers are tasked solely with verification, measurement, and classification. We establish that the attention occurred. We measure its duration. We classify its type. We issue the certificate of registry.
Our clerks and field officers operate in situations of profound human depletion. Attention is often born of anxiety, obligation, or grief. You must treat every petitioner with absolute procedural seriousness. A citizen who has spent twenty years observing a shifting property line has expended a portion of their life in a fixed direction. We honor that expenditure by recording it accurately.
— Director Elias H. Thorne, January 2024
I. OFFICIAL CATEGORIES OF ACKNOWLEDGED NOTICE
The Bureau currently recognizes six categories of directed notice. Petitions for entry into the National Ledger must be filed under one of the following designations.

1. Notice Fixed (Arrested): Sudden, unbroken orientation toward a singular emergent stimulus.
2. Notice Sustained (Obligatory): Attention maintained by social, legal, occupational, or domestic requirement over an extended duration.
3. Notice Ceremonial: Collective attention directed by a recognized civic, agricultural, or religious calendar.
4. Notice Hereditary: Transmitted patterns of vigilance passed across two or more generations regarding specific localities, structures, or events.
5. Notice Disputed (Friction): A claim of attention rendered which the recipient explicitly denies having received, or a claim of attention withheld which the subject asserts was present.
6. Notice Ambient (Vigilant): Diffuse, low-level readiness toward an environment maintained without a specific focal point.
(Note: Detailed definitions, examples, and measurement conventions for these categories are located in the Section II Taxonomy Schedule.)
II. VERIFICATION PROCEDURE
When a petitioner submits Form 11-A (Assertion of Notice), the Bureau undertakes a standard verification process. Notice is an invisible expenditure; therefore, the Bureau relies on physical, environmental, and documentary evidence of its occurrence.

1. Site Inspection: A Field Officer must visit the site of the asserted notice. The officer will look for environmental wear consistent with prolonged presence (e.g., worn floorboards at a window, indentations on armrests, flattened grass).
2. Corroboration: The officer will collect affidavits from neighbors, co-habitants, or local authorities establishing the physical presence and orientation of the petitioner during the claimed hours.
3. Physiological Assessment: Review of optical records, posture degradation, or repetitive strain injuries consistent with the angle and duration of the claimed notice.
4. Duration Log: The petitioner must provide a plausible reconstruction of hours spent. Time spent asleep or distracted by secondary tasks must be subtracted from the total claim.
III. PERSONNEL CONDUCT
Field Officers are representatives of the historical record. Conduct must remain strictly neutral and observational.

* No Reciprocal Notice: Officers must not engage in prolonged eye contact with petitioners during the intake interview. The officer is there to record attention, not to absorb it.
* Neutrality of Object: Officers will not comment on the utility of the petitioner's focus. If a petitioner has spent four thousand hours watching a telephone pole, the officer will only confirm the angle of view and the time elapsed.
* Solicitude: Officers will offer standard-issue water and tissues during intake. Officers will not offer physical comfort. The highest form of care the Bureau provides is the accurate processing of the paperwork.
IV. HAZARDS
Field work carries occupational hazards related to proximity to concentrated attention.

* Gauger's Drift: The involuntary adoption of the petitioner's focal point. Officers must strictly avoid looking at the object of a petitioner's Sustained Notice for more than sixty seconds at a time.
* Duration Fatigue: Officers exposed to environments holding massive reserves of Hereditary Notice may experience sudden lethargy or the sensation of being physically compressed. Officers are required to take a fifteen-minute recess outside the affected structure every two hours.
* The Vacuum Effect: Encountering an individual who has received zero units of directed notice for a period exceeding a decade. This can induce profound disorientation in the evaluating officer. Standard protocol requires two officers to be present when processing a zero-notice subject.
V. WHAT THE BUREAU DOES NOT UNDERTAKE
The Bureau's mandate is strictly defined. We are an agency of the ledger. Therefore:

* The Bureau does not intervene to stop an individual from expending notice.
* The Bureau does not compel an individual to pay attention to another.
* The Bureau does not mediate romantic disputes regarding the quality of attention given.
* The Bureau does not record claims of notice directed toward the divine, the supernatural, or the deceased (unless the deceased is physically present in the room).
* The Bureau does not gauge the interior thoughts of the petitioner, only the physical direction and duration of their focus.
OFFICIAL TAXONOMY AND MEASUREMENT SCHEDULE
The following schedule governs the classification of all petitions filed with the Bureau. An instance of notice must meet the definitional requirements and measurement conventions of its category to be entered into the Primary Ledger.
Class I: Notice Fixed (Arrested)

* Definition: Sudden, unbroken physical and sensory orientation toward a singular, emergent stimulus in the immediate environment.
* Examples: Observing a building collapse; watching a stranger fall in a crosswalk; staring at a sudden alteration in weather.
* Measurement Convention: Gaugeable. Measured in standard seconds and minutes. The clock begins at the moment of physical orientation (head turn, cessation of walking) and terminates at the subject's first lateral eye movement away from the stimulus.
Class II: Notice Sustained (Obligatory)

* Definition: Attention maintained upon a specific subject or object over an extended duration due to social, legal, occupational, or domestic requirement.
* Examples: A sentry monitoring a gate; a juror observing a witness; a parent watching a child in a public swimming pool.
* Measurement Convention: Gaugeable. Measured in total hours logged, minus a standardized deduction of 12% for involuntary physiological lapses (blinking, momentary distraction, biological needs).
Class III: Notice Ceremonial

* Definition: Collective attention directed toward a focal point as dictated by a recognized civic, agricultural, or religious calendar.
* Examples: A municipal moment of silence; watching a funeral procession; observing a scheduled eclipse.
* Measurement Convention: Gaugeable by aggregate. Measured by multiplying the verified headcount of the assembly by the prescribed duration of the ceremony. The resulting figure is logged as a single block of "Civic Expenditure."
Class IV: Notice Hereditary

* Definition: Transmitted patterns of vigilance regarding specific localities, structures, or events, passed sequentially across two or more generations of a single family or organization.
* Examples: Monitoring a specific section of a treeline due to a grandfather's previous habit; checking a basement foundation for dampness at a specific hour; observing a defunct industrial facility.
* Measurement Convention: Partially gaugeable. Total duration cannot be strictly verified. The Bureau measures this category by verifying genealogical continuity and confirming the existence of inherited instructional material (diaries, verbal directives corroborated by witnesses). Entered into the ledger as "Multi-Generational Continuous."
Class V: Notice Disputed (Friction)

* Definition: A formal claim of attention rendered by a provider, which the designated recipient explicitly denies having received; or a claim of attention withheld by a provider, which the subject asserts was present.
* Examples: A teacher claiming they monitored a student's progress, while the student claims they were ignored; a spouse claiming they noticed a change in the home, while the partner disputes the claim.
* Measurement Convention: Ungaugeable by direct duration. Gaugeable only by the absence of interrupting conduct. The officer must investigate the environmental record to determine if the provider was physically capable of rendering the notice during the disputed timeframe. If capability is proven, the petition is marked "Registered - Contested."
Class VI: Notice Ambient (Vigilant)

* Definition: Diffuse, low-level sensory readiness directed toward a general environment without a singular, fixed focal point.
* Examples: Walking alone through an unlit parking garage; a mother sleeping while listening for a child's cough; an employee working in the presence of a volatile supervisor.
* Measurement Convention: Ungaugeable. Ambient notice cannot be quantified in hours due to its diffuse nature. However, the Bureau acknowledges its existence. Petitions under this class are filed in the Secondary Ledger and do not receive a certificate of duration, only a certificate of acknowledgment.
DOSSIER 2011-804-HN
CATEGORY: Notice Hereditary (Class IV)
PETITIONER: Arthur Penhaligon (b. 1952)
VERIFYING OFFICER: Field Clerk Miriam Dawes (Badge #4012)
LOCATION: 114 County Road 9, Owyhee County, Idaho
DATE OF INTAKE: October 14, 2011
PROCEDURAL BACKGROUND
On September 2, 2011, the Bureau received Form 11-A from Arthur Penhaligon, requesting the formal registration of ninety-four (94) years of Hereditary Notice. The object of this notice was a dry irrigation ditch running parallel to the eastern border of the petitioner's property.
The petitioner stated that the property was being sold to a commercial agricultural conglomerate on November 1, 2011, and that the house would be leveled. The petitioner, having no heirs, stated he would be relocating to a municipal care facility in Boise. He filed the petition to ensure that the accumulated attention paid to the ditch by his grandfather (1917–1955), his father (1955–1988), and himself (1988–2011) was entered into the National Ledger before the physical site was destroyed.
FIELD OBSERVATIONS
On October 14, 2011, I arrived at the property at 0900 hours. The petitioner, Mr. Penhaligon, was cooperative and exhibited a standard level of exhaustion common to terminal petitioners.
I conducted a walk-through of the primary residence. The locus of observation was determined to be a second-story rear bedroom window facing east.
Physical Evidence of Notice:

1. The linoleum flooring directly beneath the east-facing window exhibited severe degradation, worn entirely through to the subfloor in two distinct oval shapes consistent with standing adult footwear.
2. The wooden window sill displayed a shallow, polished depression on the right-hand side, measuring four inches in length. I tested the depression. It perfectly accommodated the human elbow.
3. The sash cords of the window had been replaced multiple times, indicating frequent opening.
4. The exterior view provided an unobstructed line of sight to the dry irrigation ditch, located two hundred yards from the residence.
Interview with Petitioner:
I asked Mr. Penhaligon to detail the nature of the notice. He stated that in 1917, his grandfather, Elias Penhaligon, began watching the ditch every evening between the hours of 1700 and 1900. When asked what his grandfather was looking for, the petitioner stated, "He never said. He just said it needed to be kept an eye on."
The petitioner provided diaries belonging to his father, Thomas Penhaligon. An entry dated April 14, 1955, reads: Dad passed at noon. I took the window at five o'clock. Nothing in the ditch.
The petitioner confirmed he assumed the duty in 1988 upon his father's stroke. He stated he has stood at the window from 1700 to 1900 hours every day for twenty-three years. He reported that he has never seen water, wildlife, or human activity in the ditch during this time.
I asked the petitioner why he continued the practice. He replied, "Because if I didn't look, the time my father and grandfather spent looking would just be dead time. I had to finish the line."
ASSESSMENT
The Bureau does not require an object of notice to perform an action. The Bureau does not require the notice to be useful. The Bureau only requires that the notice was directed and sustained.
The physical evidence of the floorboards and the windowsill strongly corroborates the claim of ninety-four years of concentrated, daily observation. The transmission of the duty from grandfather to father to son is thoroughly documented in the family records. The chain of notice is unbroken.
OUTCOME AND RULING
STATUS: GRANTED.
The petition is approved. The Bureau officially recognizes 68,620 hours of directed attention (two hours daily x 365 days x 94 years) applied to the eastern irrigation ditch at 114 County Road 9.
This total has been entered into the Primary Ledger under Hereditary Notice.
Addendum by Officer Dawes: I presented Mr. Penhaligon with the blue Certificate of Registry at the conclusion of the inspection. He folded it carefully, placed it in his breast pocket, and walked to his kitchen table. He did not look out the east window for the remainder of my visit. The petitioner's duty is discharged. Case closed.
GLOSSARY OF INTERNAL TERMINOLOGY
The following terms are standardized vocabulary for Bureau personnel and are strictly utilized in internal memorandums and official rulings.
Anchor: A physical object (e.g., a photograph, a horizon line, a specific chair) utilized by a subject to maintain Notice Sustained without physiological drifting.
Blind Expenditure: An instance of intense, prolonged attention directed toward a recipient that the provider falsely believes to be present (e.g., watching a window assuming someone is behind the curtain). Recorded only in the Secondary Ledger.
Lapse-Time: The quantifiable duration between the involuntary breaking of attention and its deliberate re-establishment by the subject.
Observer's Recess: The mandatory fifteen-minute physical withdrawal from a site of heavily concentrated attention, required of Field Officers to prevent occupational fatigue.
Phantom Notice: The sensation reported by a recipient of being heavily observed when Bureau instruments and investigations confirm no human observer is physically present or actively registered.
Recipient Void: A physical subject, structure, or geographic coordinate that the Bureau has verified has received absolutely no directed human notice for a period exceeding thirty consecutive years.
Saccade Debt: The accumulated ocular and cognitive fatigue recorded in subjects following extreme durations of Notice Fixed (Arrested).
Spillover: Accidental, undocumented attention granted to an adjacent object, person, or structure when the primary object of focus is suddenly removed from the environment.
Terminal Gaze: The final verified instance of attention paid to an object before that object is destroyed, lost, or permanently obscured from human sight.
