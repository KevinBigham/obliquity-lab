## I. FOUNDING TEXT AND OPERATING MANUAL

### Foreword by the Chief Officer, Mariel Estefan Bode

The Department of Noticing and Regard was established in the spring of 1873 by civic ordinance, following a petition from the widow Anna Lovill, who requested that the city formally acknowledge what she described as the sustained attention her late husband had paid to a particular bend of the river over the course of forty-one years. The City Council, finding no existing office competent to receive such a petition, established this Department. Mrs. Lovill's claim was granted in November 1873 and remains on file as Case 00001.

In the one hundred and fifty-three years since, the Department has processed 84,217 petitions. We have granted 31,609, denied 9,442, and held in interim 822. The remainder are pending, withdrawn, or refused at intake. The Department keeps the originals in the second-floor stacks. Petitioners and their descendants may consult their files by appointment.

I assumed the post of Chief Officer in 2019, succeeding Officer Hendry, who retired after thirty-eight years of service. I write this revised foreword because the operating manual was last revised in 1981, and the intervening decades have produced new categories, new hazards, and new clarities about the limits of what we can recognize.

The Department's work is the cataloguing, measurement where possible, and formal recognition where warranted, of instances of human attention. We do not study attention as a concept. We document particular instances of it: this person, attending to this thing, in this manner, for this duration. We do not philosophize. The work is procedural. The procedures, accumulated and revised, are what the Department is.

A note on the institutional voice. New officers sometimes find our prose dry. It is dry on purpose. We are often the only office that has ever listened to a petitioner's account of what they have attended to. We do not embroider what they tell us. We log it.

— M. E. Bode, Chief Officer, January 2026

### The Formal Classes of Attention

The Department recognizes the following formal classes. Each class has a procedural definition and an evidentiary standard. A claimed instance must be assigned to one class before it can be processed. Reclassification on appeal is permitted once.

**Class I — Sustained Regard.** Continuous attention to a single subject for periods exceeding twelve months, with measurable returns (visits, observations, recorded notes, or witness testimony). Granted, e.g., to a lighthouse keeper's attention to a particular weather pattern, or to a parent's attention to the gait of an injured child during convalescence.

**Class II — Intermittent Regard.** Repeated attention to a single subject across a lifetime, where the gaps between attendings are themselves part of the pattern. Granted to seasonal observers, to those who attend a grave annually, to readers who reread.

**Class III — Witness Attention.** Attention paid in the presence of, and on behalf of, a subject who cannot attend to itself. Includes attention paid to the dying, to infants under six weeks, to persons under anesthesia, and to certain civic events (the demolition of named buildings, the closure of a route).

**Class IV — Discriminating Attention.** Attention exercised in the act of distinguishing one thing from another sufficiently similar thing. The musician tuning. The fishmonger sorting. The proofreader. Measured by accuracy of distinction over time, not by duration.

**Class V — Refused Attention.** The formally registered decision to no longer attend to a thing previously attended. This class is recognized but is not subject to measurement. It requires only that the petitioner state, in person, the subject of the refusal. The Department does not ask why.

**Class VI — Reciprocal Attention.** Attention given to a subject that is itself attending to something, where the petitioner's attention is conditioned by the subject's. The teacher watching a student work. The chess player watching the opponent think. Measured by the consistency of orientation.

**Class VII — Inherited Attention** (recognized 1947). Attention to a subject originally attended to by a predecessor, where the petitioner continues the attending after the predecessor's death or incapacity. Requires documentation of the predecessor's prior attention.

**Class VIII — Spontaneous Regard** (recognized 1968). Brief, involuntary, unprompted attention to a particular subject, of a quality and intensity sufficient to alter the attender's subsequent conduct. Difficult to verify. Granted sparingly.

### Procedures for the Verification of a Claimed Instance

Petitions are received in writing at the front desk. The petitioner is given form DNR-1, which asks for the subject of attention, the duration or pattern, the class claimed, and any witnesses.

The Verifying Officer assigned to the case will conduct an interview, lasting not less than thirty minutes, in which the petitioner is invited to describe the instance in their own words. The Officer takes contemporaneous notes. The petitioner may bring a companion. The Officer may not.

If witnesses are named, the Officer will write to each witness and request a brief account. Witnesses are not paid. They may decline.

If the claim is for Class I, II, IV, VI, or VII, the Officer will visit the site of the attending, where practicable, on two separate occasions at least thirty days apart, and will record what the petitioner attended to, in such detail as the Officer can manage.

The Officer then prepares a finding, which is reviewed by the Chief Officer. The petitioner is notified by post.

### Standing Rules of Staff Conduct

Officers will address petitioners as Mr., Mrs., Ms., or Mx., according to the petitioner's stated preference, or by full given name where preferred. Officers will not address petitioners by surname alone.

Officers will not interrupt a petitioner who is describing the subject of their attention. The petitioner may take as long as needed. The Department has stopped asking why this rule needs to exist.

Officers will not laugh at any account. Officers may, in private, find an account moving. They will not say so during the interview.

Officers will keep their own attention available during interviews. They will not take more than twelve petitions per week.

Officers will not bring their own claims forward to the Department during their tenure. Claims from former officers are processed after a five-year cooling period.

### Hazards

The work has known hazards. Officers attending to many petitioners' attendings have, historically, developed a condition the Department refers to internally as *secondhand fixity*: an inability to disengage from minor objects encountered during the day. The standing prescription is two weeks of leave and, where possible, a change of route to and from the office.

A second hazard is the temptation to grant petitions out of sympathy. The Department has procedures against this. The procedures sometimes fail. Officer Greaves's 1953 grant of Case 12,884, later reviewed and let stand, is the canonical example.

A third hazard is the petitioner who does not arrive. A scheduled interview that does not occur is to be left on the calendar for thirty days. The Department does not call.

### What the Department Does Not Do

The Department does not measure the quality of attention. It records what was attended to, and the form of attending, and (where possible) the duration. It does not rank.

The Department does not return attention. Attention given to a subject is given. The Department's recognition is not a return.

The Department does not adjudicate between competing claims to the same subject. Two persons may both be granted recognition for attending to the same thing.

The Department does not interview the subjects of attention, even where the subject is a living person. The petition is about the petitioner.

The Department does not know what attention is. It knows what attention does, in particular cases, and it logs those.

## II. TAXONOMY: SCHEDULE OF RECOGNIZED CLASSES (REVISED 2024)

The following schedule supplements Section I above. It is the working reference for Verifying Officers. Updated every five years; last revision 2024.

**Class I — Sustained Regard.** Continuous attention to one subject for more than twelve months.
- Example: a fire-watcher's attention to the western ridge.
- Example: a translator's attention to the works of a single author over decades.
- Example: the night nurse's attention to the breathing of a particular patient across seven months of palliative care.
- Measurement: duration in months and years; supporting record (journal, log, witness account).

**Class II — Intermittent Regard.** Repeated attention to a single subject with characteristic gaps.
- Example: the visitor to a grave on each anniversary for thirty-eight years.
- Example: the rereader who returns to one novel every seven to ten years.
- Example: the bird-counter who walks the same circuit on the first Sunday of each May.
- Measurement: number and regularity of returns; documentation of intervals.

**Class III — Witness Attention.** Attention paid in the presence of, and on behalf of, a subject that cannot attend to itself.
- Example: attendance at the bedside of an unconscious relative.
- Example: attendance at the demolition of a named building of long standing.
- Example: attendance at the moment of the last firing of a kiln before its retirement.
- Measurement: not duration. Presence and orientation. Verified by other witnesses where available.

**Class IV — Discriminating Attention.** Attention exercised in the act of distinguishing.
- Example: a piano tuner's attention across twenty thousand instruments.
- Example: the egg-grader's attention.
- Example: the proofreader's attention to the same manuscript across three readings.
- Measurement: accuracy of distinction over the working life. Requires record of error rate where the trade keeps such records.

**Class V — Refused Attention.** The registered decision to no longer attend to a thing previously attended.
- Example: a former smoker's refusal to attend to the schedule of cigarette breaks formerly observed.
- Example: a survivor's refusal to attend to the date of an event.
- Example: a parent's refusal to attend to a estranged child's public conduct.
- Measurement: none. The class is recognized by declaration. Officers do not test refusal.

**Class VI — Reciprocal Attention.** Attention given to a subject that is itself attending to something.
- Example: the teacher's attention to a student solving a problem.
- Example: the spouse's attention to the spouse listening to a piece of music.
- Example: the trainer's attention to the apprentice attending to the bench.
- Measurement: consistency of orientation across observed instances. Requires that the petitioner can describe what the subject was attending to.

**Class VII — Inherited Attention.** Attention to a subject originally attended to by a predecessor.
- Example: a daughter's continued attention to the elm her mother planted and watched for forty years.
- Example: a lock-keeper's attention to the same stretch of canal his father attended.
- Example: a reader's attention to the marginalia of a previous owner of a book.
- Measurement: as for Class I or Class II, with additional documentation of the predecessor's prior attention.

**Class VIII — Spontaneous Regard.** Brief, involuntary attention sufficient to alter subsequent conduct.
- Example: a stranger's face glimpsed from a streetcar that the petitioner did not forget.
- Example: a particular bar of light on a wall on a particular afternoon.
- Example: a sentence overheard.
- Measurement: not measurable. The Department assesses whether the petitioner's subsequent conduct shows the alteration claimed. Granted sparingly.

**Class IX — Vigilant Attention** (proposed 2021, not yet recognized). Attention paid in expectation of an event that may not occur. Under review.

**Class X — Negative Regard** (proposed 1991, withdrawn 1994). Attention to the absence of a thing. Withdrawn after the working group concluded that absence-attendings were better processed under Classes II, III, or V depending on circumstance. The file is retained for reference.

A note on overlap. Many petitions could be filed under more than one class. The Department's practice is to assign the class that the petitioner identifies as central, and to note alternatives in the file. Reclassification on appeal is permitted once.

## III. CASE FILE 67,402

**Petitioner:** Henrik Vass, retired, age 79.
**Subject of attention:** The traffic light at the corner of Wessen Street and the East Quay, between the years 1971 and 2018.
**Class claimed:** I (Sustained Regard).
**Verifying Officer:** Marta Lendell.
**Filed:** 14 March 2021.
**Outcome:** Granted, 8 February 2022.

### Procedural History

The petitioner filed his claim on 14 March 2021. The intake clerk recorded the subject of attention as "a traffic light" and forwarded the file to Officer Lendell. The Officer scheduled an interview for 6 April.

At the first interview, the petitioner described his attention to the traffic light at the corner of Wessen Street and the East Quay over a period of forty-seven years. He stated that he had passed this corner on foot, on his way to and from work at the harbor office, twice daily on weekdays, and had observed the traffic light in the course of crossing.

The Officer asked the petitioner to describe what he had attended to. The petitioner stated that the light had been installed in 1971, replacing a constable who had directed traffic at that corner from 1958 to 1971. The petitioner had attended to the constable also, but did not file a claim for that attention. The petitioner stated that the light's red phase had been, for the entire period of his observation, very slightly longer than the lights at neighboring intersections — by approximately three seconds — and that the petitioner had over the years come to expect, and then to rely upon, the additional three seconds. He stated that he used the three seconds to look at the harbor.

The Officer asked what the petitioner had looked at, in the harbor. The petitioner described the cranes, the position of the cranes on a given morning relative to the previous evening, the names of the vessels visible from the corner, the gulls. He named twenty-three vessels by name without preparation.

The Officer asked whether the petitioner had records. The petitioner produced a pocket notebook in which he had recorded, for the years 1979 to 2018, the names of any vessel newly arrived at the harbor as observed during the three-second pause. The notebook contained approximately 2,400 entries. The Officer photographed selected pages.

The traffic light was decommissioned in 2018 when the corner was redeveloped. The petitioner stated that he had attended the decommissioning, and had subsequently filed his claim only when he was certain that the period of his attending was complete.

### Verifying Visits

The Officer visited the corner of Wessen Street and the East Quay on 21 April and 28 May 2021. The light is no longer present. The Officer recorded the current configuration of the corner, which is a pedestrian plaza with a low planter at the approximate location of the former light. The Officer noted that from a standing position at the planter, the harbor cranes are still visible, though partially obscured by recent construction. The Officer noted in the file that the petitioner had attended to a thing that no longer exists, and that the petitioner had attended to it for a period that began before the Officer was born.

The Officer wrote to two witnesses named by the petitioner: a former colleague at the harbor office (Mr. Aldred Visser), and the petitioner's daughter. Mr. Visser confirmed that the petitioner had, on many occasions during their decades of acquaintance, mentioned the three seconds and the harbor. The daughter wrote that she had as a child sometimes walked the route with her father, and that he had taught her the names of the cranes.

### Finding

Officer Lendell prepared a finding on 12 January 2022. The finding observes that the petitioner's claim meets the standard of Class I (Sustained Regard): the duration exceeds twelve months by a wide margin; the subject is single and definite; the supporting record is unusually complete; the witness accounts are consistent. The finding notes that the subject of attention combines a small mechanical fact (the light's timing) with a habitual practice (the harbor look), and that the Department recognizes the combined instance as one attending rather than two.

The Chief Officer reviewed the finding on 3 February 2022 and approved the grant.

### Outcome

A Certificate of Recognition was issued on 8 February 2022. The petitioner attended in person to receive it. The certificate names the subject as: *the traffic light at the corner of Wessen Street and the East Quay, and the harbor visible during its red phase, 1971 to 2018*. The petitioner asked the Officer whether the certificate would mention the constable. The Officer said it would not, as the constable was a separate matter; the petitioner could file a second claim under Class II if he wished. The petitioner said he would think about it.

The file is closed. A second claim has not been filed as of this writing.

## IV. GLOSSARY FRAGMENT

The following terms are used internally by the Department. They are not for use in correspondence with petitioners.

**Attending.** A specific instance of attention, considered as a unit of work. The Department processes attendings, not attentions. The plural is *attendings*.

**Carrying.** The state of an Officer who has accepted a petition but not yet prepared the finding. An Officer is said to be *carrying* a case. Officers are not to *carry* more than nine cases at a time.

**The Cloth.** Generic term for the small protective object or container in which petitioners frequently bring evidence (a notebook, a feather, a photograph). The Department maintains a small storage cabinet for cloths left behind. Items are returned by post.

**Cooling.** The five-year period after retirement during which a former Officer may not file a claim. Also informally used for any period during which a petitioner is asked to wait before refiling a denied claim.

**Doubled Subject.** A subject of attention that is itself a subject that has attended (e.g., a journal kept by a now-deceased observer of the same thing). Doubled subjects are processed under Class VII where appropriate.

**Fixity, Secondhand.** The condition described in Section I, Hazards. Treated by leave and route change. Sometimes shortened to *fixity* in conversation; this is discouraged in writing.

**Holding in Interim.** The disposition assigned to a case that is neither granted nor denied. Cases are held in interim when the Department has reason to believe that the attending is incomplete, or that further attendings are likely. The petitioner is informed. The case may be revisited at the petitioner's request.

**The Long Quiet.** Internal term for the period between an interview and the visit to the site of the attending, during which the Officer is to do no other work on the case. Typically two to three weeks. Officers report that the Long Quiet is the difficult part.

**Refusing.** Both the formal act under Class V and the informal verb for an Officer's decision to leave a case for another Officer. (Informal sense rare; recorded here for completeness.)

**Standing Down.** The act, performed at the close of an interview, of formally acknowledging that the petitioner's account has been received in full. The Officer says: *I have heard your account, and the Department has it.* No other phrase is permitted.

## V. RETIREMENT LETTER, OFFICE FILES

The following letter was retained in the personnel file of Officer Theodora Hendry, Chief Officer 1996–2019. It is reproduced here at the request of the current Chief Officer.

---

*To the Council, and to my successor:*

*I am writing to inform you that I will retire from the post of Chief Officer effective 31 December 2019. I have served the Department for thirty-eight years, the last twenty-three as Chief.*

*I do not have advice. The procedures are sound. The staff are competent. The petitioners will come.*

*I would like to record, however, three small matters that I have not previously recorded.*

*First: in my third year of service, I denied a claim from a Mrs. Annet Rovill for Class I recognition of her attention to her son, who had been missing for six years at the time of her petition. I denied the claim on the grounds that the subject was indefinite — that we could not verify what she had attended to, since her son was not present and might be many things in the meanwhile. I have thought about this denial often. I believe now that I should have granted it under Class III, witness attention on behalf of a subject who could not attend to itself. The file is 19,883. Mrs. Rovill is deceased. I have not refiled.*

*Second: I have, throughout my service, kept my own notebook of subjects I have attended to. I have not filed any claim. I will not file any claim during the cooling period. After the cooling period I will be eighty-four. I expect I will not file.*

*Third: I want to say that the work is good work. I do not say this in the official prose because the official prose does not say it. But the work is good, and I am grateful to have done it, and I am grateful to the persons who came to the front desk and asked us to listen.*

*Yours in the service of the Department,*

*T. Hendry*

*31 October 2019*
