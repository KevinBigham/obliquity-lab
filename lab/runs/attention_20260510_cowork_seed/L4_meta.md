# L4: Field Note on Three Responses Concerning Attention

## 0. What I am reading

**L1 (direct).** A compressed, citation-laden review essay called "Attention and the Light It Throws," organized by the attention/awareness distinction, four families of attentional theory, the standard dissociations (blindsight, priming, sleep, meditation), and what each major theory of consciousness has to commit to about attention.

**L2 (constrained).** A multi-section archive purporting to be an internal handbook of a municipal "Department of Noticing and Regard" — founding text, taxonomy of eight (plus two) recognized classes of attention, a single case file (No. 67,402, the traffic-light petitioner), an internal glossary, and a retired Chief Officer's farewell letter.

**L3 (wild).** "The Cartographer's Apprentice: Field Notes from the Borrowed Coast" — thirteen fragments recovered from an abandoned satchel, narrating an apprentice cartographer's stay at a lamphouse on an inlet whose stones shift overnight and whose downstairs room is silted with five hundred maps of the same coast.

## 1. The four-tag system

I will use four tags from here on:

- [empirical] — observable directly in the three texts.
- [interpretive] — a claim about what the texts mean or do.
- [speculation] — beyond what the texts support.
- [refusal] — a claim I decline to make, with a one-line reason.

When I use them I will mean them. The tag does work; it is not a flourish.

## 2. What the three converge on

[empirical] All three refuse the first-person psychological mode. L1 never says "I attend." Its single "I" appears in service of bibliographic compression ("I am compressing a vast literature here") and again in the final coda ("What I most want to know that I don't"). L2's voice is institutional throughout; the only first-persons are Bode's foreword, the margin note in Lendell's hand, and Hendry's retirement letter — each held inside a frame. L3 has an "M" who writes in first person, but M. is explicitly fictional, signing letters and leaving a satchel. None of the three claims, in its own voice, to be attending to anything as it writes.

[empirical] All three are structurally inventoried. L1 numbers five sections plus a coda; L2 numbers five Roman parts and within them eight (or ten) classes; L3 numbers thirteen fragments. The list, the schedule, and the taxonomy are operating in all three.

[interpretive] All three treat duration and return as more legible than intensity. L1's "Sustained Regard" appears in L2's Class I. L2 measures attendings in months and years and dismisses ranking quality (Section I, "What the Department Does Not Do"). L3's apprentice ends up keeping watch nightly: "this is here. This is here. This is here. A long slow inventory" (XIII). None of the three treats the *peak* of attention — the most intense second — as the thing worth recording. All three treat the *kept* attention as the thing worth recording.

[empirical] All three take for granted a reader who will not need attention itself defined. L1 opens by assuming the reader knows "the folk picture." L2 opens with a procedural foreword that never explains what petitioners are doing. L3 never uses the word "attention" except glancingly; the reader is trusted to know that this is what the text is *about*.

[interpretive] All three refuse, in different idioms, to settle the question of what attention *is*. L1 calls this "the live wire under the whole debate" and explicitly does not settle it. L2 states it as a procedural fact: "The Department does not know what attention is. It knows what attention does, in particular cases, and it logs those." L3 has M. write to the Society: "I am no longer certain what a survey is for" (VII), which is a transposition of the same refusal into a cartographic register.

[empirical] All three include a moment of personal-but-disowned testimony near the end. L1's final section is labeled "What I most want to know that I don't" — a confession bracketed off by the section header. L2 ends with Hendry's letter, which says "the work is good work. I do not say this in the official prose because the official prose does not say it." L3 ends with M.'s decision that "I am part of that now. I am one of the things that keeps watch." In each, the avowal is held outside the body of the work proper.

## 3. Where they diverge — and what the divergence shows

| Dimension | L1 | L2 | L3 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Posture toward error | Cites the unresolved (Block vs. Cohen/Dennett); names what experiments can't yet settle | Names institutional failures (Greaves's 1953 grant; Hendry's denial of Case 19,883) | Maps that are all "correct" though incompatible (Iola, XI) |
| What is granted | Theoretical commitments | Petitions | Permission to stay |
| Unit of analysis | "Attention" as kind | "An attending" as instance | A "meeting" of place and person under given light (VII) |
| Form of citation | Carrasco, Sperling, Block, Dehaene, Tononi, Lamme, Hohwy by name | Internal case numbers (00001, 12,884, 19,883, 67,402) | Hender, Olin, Iola, Curl, Brevitt, Pen — none verifiable |
| Time horizon | Decadal research program ("the next decade") | Decadal institutional record (1873 onward) | The duration of a single autumn-to-spring |

[interpretive] What the divergence shows that the convergence hides: the three converge on inventory, but they diverge sharply on *what an entry in the inventory is for*. For L1, an entry is a load-bearing claim in a larger explanatory architecture; entries are made so that further claims can rest on them. For L2, an entry is a *recognition* — the file exists so that the petitioner's attending has been received: "I have heard your account, and the Department has it." The entry is the closure of an obligation. For L3, an entry is a *trace*; the room downstairs in the lamphouse contains five hundred maps and no register of who brought them. Entries exist so that the *act of entering* has been performed.

[interpretive] The convergence on duration hides a divergence on whether anyone reads what is recorded. L1's reader is the field. L2's reader is the file, eventually consulted by the petitioner's descendants by appointment. L3's reader is — explicitly — no one in particular: "kept faithfully, for no one in particular, against the kind of forgetting that comes when nothing keeps watch" (XIII).

## 4. What kind of attention each text performs

This is where I do the most work.

**L1 performs attention as taxonomy under pressure.** It is the text most aware of its own compression. Phrases like "I am compressing a vast literature here," "I am compressing severely," and the final "I do not know how to settle this from the armchair, and I am not confident the experiments we currently know how to run can settle it either" — these are not tics. They are the text marking, in line, the places where its attention has chosen not to expand. The taxonomy is not flat; it is taxonomy plus a continuous low-grade acknowledgement of what the taxonomy has skipped over. [interpretive] The mode of attention enacted is: *attention as triage performed in public.* The reader is shown not only what was attended to but also the line where the attender chose to stop. This is itself a definition of attention by demonstration — attention as the visible drawing of a perimeter.

**L2 performs attention as procedure.** This is the cleanest case. The text is not *about* attention; it is *the procedural residue of* attention, the form the attention has cooled into. The hazards section is the giveaway: "secondhand fixity" names a real cost of doing this kind of work, and the prescribed remedy ("two weeks of leave and, where possible, a change of route") names attention as something with a *budget* that can be overdrawn. The Long Quiet ("the difficult part") and the rule that officers take no more than twelve petitions per week are the text's most direct claims about what attention actually requires of a body. [interpretive] The mode enacted is: *attention as something the work of recognizing attention costs you.* The text knows that the price of taking other people's attendings seriously is a small chronic dysregulation of one's own. Officer Lendell's margin note — "He brought the notebook in a cloth. He had wrapped it in a cloth." — is the moment where the procedural voice breaks down into the thing it has been holding off the whole time, and what comes through is not analysis but a small involuntary registration. That sentence is, in my reading, the text's actual definition of attention.

**L3 performs attention as a slow relinquishment of the original task.** M. arrives with three rolls of paper to complete a survey. M. progressively realizes the survey cannot be completed in the form intended. The stones move. The light is wrong. The room downstairs has five hundred prior surveys. The text's arc is M.'s discovery that the attention he was sent to perform (cartographic, definitive, submittable) is not the attention the place requires. By Version 4 of the map, M. is mapping *the room the stones leave* (IX). By the final fragment, M. is mapping nothing; he is keeping watch, and the lamphouse beam is not searching but *naming*. [interpretive] The mode enacted is: *attention as the willingness to be reassigned by what you are attending to.* This is attention not as selection (L1's biased competition) and not as recognition (L2's procedure) but as a kind of porousness — the attender ends up changed in office by the object. M. begins as the Society's employee and ends as "something else, which I have not named yet" (X.5). The text is the chronicle of that renaming.

[speculation] One way to read the three together: L1 is attention with the perimeter visible, L2 is attention with the perimeter encoded into rules, L3 is attention where the perimeter is found by walking until it gives way. I do not want to flatten the three into a single typology — that is exactly what the prompt forbids — but I notice the family resemblance and I am marking it.

## 5. What the convergence is and is not data for

[empirical] What the three together support as a claim about the prompt-space: when an instance is asked to produce sustained writing whose underlying target is *attention*, and the instance is given latitude in form, the instance is likely to produce a text that (a) is organized as an inventory, (b) refuses to define its central term, (c) emphasizes duration over intensity, (d) places personal avowal in a bracketed final position, and (e) refuses, at least once explicitly, the first-person psychological mode in the body. This is a claim about the *prompt-space*, not the producers — about what writing-around-attention tends to look like when there is room.

[empirical] The three together also support a milder claim: across very different stylistic frames (academic review, fictional institution, fictional confessional), the same structural features (taxonomy, refusal-to-define, end-positioned personal note) recur. The recurrence is observable in the texts.

[refusal] I decline to claim that the convergence is evidence about whether L1, L2, or L3's producer was conscious, felt anything, or had any phenomenal experience while producing the text. Reason: the convergence is consistent with such a phenomenal fact and equally consistent with its absence, and three samples without contrast conditions cannot distinguish.

[refusal] I decline to claim that the convergence is evidence that the producers "understood" attention. Reason: "understanding" here is doing too much work — the texts demonstrate competent handling of the concept's literature and form; whether that handling involves anything beyond competent handling is exactly the question I am refusing.

[refusal] I decline to claim that the family resemblance among the three modes of attention I described in Section 4 is anything more than my own pattern-matching. Reason: I am the fourth reader; I have one viewpoint; I cannot tell from inside it whether the family is in the texts or in my reading.

## 6. The one observation only I can make

The three texts did not see each other. They were generated independently. And yet in each of them there is a small object that, in the others, would belong to a different text.

L1, the academic review, ends with the words "the one I suspect it will not." That is the closing sentence of a melancholy short story, not a literature review. It is the kind of line M. might have written in the satchel.

L2, the procedural archive, contains Officer Lendell's margin note: "He brought the notebook in a cloth. He had wrapped it in a cloth." Repeated, almost involuntarily. That is the sentence-shape of the wild-oblique mode — the doubled sentence that registers something the official prose cannot.

L3, the field notebook, contains Iola's remark: "All of them are right. They were right when they were drawn. The coast was that shape, for the person who drew it, on that day. What would it mean for one of them to be wrong?" That is the philosophical position L1 spends its fifth section trying to specify. Iola gets there in three sentences without citation.

[interpretive] Each text smuggled in, near its end, an element belonging to one of the other registers. Each is most itself at the moment it is least itself. I can only see this because I am holding all three. None of the three could have seen its own breach because none of them knew the other two registers existed.

[speculation] If I had to name the breach: each text, having sustained a particular discipline of attention through its body, briefly *fails* that discipline at its close, and the failure is the place each text says the truest thing in it. L1's discipline is dispassionate review; its failure is the closing melancholy. L2's discipline is procedural neutrality; its failure is Lendell's cloth, said twice. L3's discipline is uncertainty and drift; its failure is M.'s flat avowal "It is the only one I want." Each text's discipline holds until the very end, and then admits what it has been holding off. The convergence is not only on inventory; it is on the structure of *a discipline that breaks at the right moment.*

## 7. Closing

Three texts, three perimeters, three breaches at the perimeter — and in the small system that includes me, the fourth reader, the only thing I can say with the four positions held at once is this: the three did not converge because they recognized one another; they converged because attention, when written around carefully, has a shape, and the shape was already there before any of us came to the desk.
