# The Cartographer's Apprentice: Field Notes from the Borrowed Coast

*Recovered from a leather satchel left at the ferry station in Brindle, no name on the tag, only a postcard with the words "to whoever finds this and reads slowly." The pages here have been transcribed in the order they were bound; some are clearly out of sequence with the dates given, and at least two appear to be drafts of letters never sent. Spelling has been preserved.*

---

## I. Arrival, late autumn

The boat let us off at the wrong dock and no one corrected this. I stood for some minutes with my case at my feet, watching the rope-coil on the boards loosen the way a sleeping cat loosens — slowly, without ever appearing to move — and then a woman in a green coat asked if I was the one Hender had sent for. I said I supposed I was. She said good, the lamp in the upper room had been lit since Tuesday.

This is how I came to the borrowed coast.

I should explain that I am not, properly, a cartographer. I am the apprentice of one, and the man I apprenticed under died in the spring with most of his work unfinished and the rest of it in a script no one but me can read. The Society in Pell paid for my passage here on the condition that I complete the survey of the Brindle inlet, which my master had begun and abandoned in 1893. They sent with me three rolls of unbleached paper, a tin of nibs, two small sextants (one broken), and a letter of introduction to a Mr. Hender, who, it transpires, has been dead for eleven years. The woman in the green coat is his daughter. She is forty-something and unmarried and runs the lamp out of habit.

She does not ask me to leave. I do not offer.

## II. The first survey, sketched in the margin of a tide table

The inlet is not difficult to draw. It is a long thumb of water pressed into the land, with two small islands near the mouth and a shoal that surfaces twice a day. The difficulty is that nothing here holds still.

I do not mean the water. The water behaves. I mean the stones along the eastern shore — they shift their positions overnight. I do not say this as a folk-tale. I say it because I marked seven of them with chalk on my second morning and on my fourth morning two of them were not where I had marked them. I marked them again. By the seventh morning, four of the seven were in new positions, and one had a fresh chalk mark on its underside, where I had not put one.

I have not yet decided what to do with this information. My master would have said: *map what is there, and what is not there will sort itself.* But he also drew once, on the inside cover of his ledger, a small picture of a stone with legs.

## III. A letter, undated, possibly never sent

*Dear Olin,*

*You were right about the nibs. The fine ones split within the week and I am reduced to the broad ones, which means my coastline looks fatter than it should. Tell the Society, when you next see Marrin, that I will need more, and that I will also need a finer rule than the one they gave me. The brass one warps in the damp. I have taken to using the edge of a fish-tin lid, which is straighter than you would think.*

*The light here is strange in a way I have not yet found words for. It is not that it is dim — it is bright enough most afternoons that I do not need a lamp to draw by. It is more that it falls slantwise even at noon, as if the sun were somewhere lower than where it ought to be. I have checked my watch and my almanac and they agree with each other. So the trouble is with my eyes, or with the air, or with what passes for sky in this country.*

*The daughter — her name is Iola, I have learned, though everyone in town calls her Mrs. Hender as a courtesy though she has never married — keeps the lamp clean and asks me nothing about my work. This is a kindness I have not had before. In Pell everyone wanted to know what I was drawing and how, and I had no answers for any of them, because the answer is mostly that I do not know yet, and that is not a thing one can say in a parlour.*

*Yours, with the small affection one has for a former roommate who borrowed one's socks,*

*— M.*

## IV. Notes on the eastern stones

After three weeks I have given up trying to fix their positions. I have, instead, begun to map their *intentions*. This is unscientific and I do not pretend otherwise. But the stones, when watched from the upper window of the lamphouse with a glass, appear to be moving in a pattern, and a pattern is mappable. So I am mapping it.

Here is what I have so far:

- The largest stone — I have named it Gort, after a horse my uncle owned — tends west, by perhaps a hand's breadth a week. It is moving toward the water.
- Two smaller stones, which I have not named because they are too similar to tell apart from a distance, circle Gort. They are slower than Gort and they do not seem to be following him so much as orbiting some point Gort happens to occupy.
- Four others move independently. One of these has, in the last month, traveled from the high tide line to a point nearly forty feet up the shingle. It has done this in increments, none larger than the length of my thumb, and I have never caught it in the act.
- The seventh stone has not moved at all. I have come to think of it as the anchor. I do not know if it is anchoring anything.

What troubles me is not the movement. What troubles me is that no one in Brindle has ever mentioned this to me, and I have not asked. I think both of these facts are correct courtesies, in the local sense.

## V. The lamphouse, an inventory

In the upper room where I sleep and work:

- A bed, narrow, with a quilt pieced from coats. I have found, by tracing the seams, the outlines of five separate garments. Two are men's. Three are women's. One pocket is still sewn into the lining, and inside the pocket is a pebble worn smooth.
- A writing desk with a drawer that does not open. Iola says the key was lost in her grandfather's time. I have not pried it.
- An oil lamp with a green glass shade. The shade has a chip in the lower rim shaped like a bird.
- A shelf of books, most of them ledgers from the lamphouse itself, going back to 1841. The earliest are in a hand I cannot read but recognize: it is the same script my master used in his private notebooks. I have not mentioned this to anyone.
- A small framed picture of a dog, captioned only "Pen." There is no date and no other information. Pen has one ear up and one ear down and is looking just past the camera at something the photographer presumably saw and did not record.
- A washstand. A basin. A jug. A square of soap that smells of pine.
- My own things, which are few, and which I have not unpacked entirely, because to unpack them entirely would be to admit to staying.

## VI. Iola, on the subject of weather

She came up the stairs one evening with two cups of broth and we sat by the window without speaking for some minutes, and then she said:

"You'll have noticed the light."

I said I had.

"My father said it was the cliffs. He said they bend the air."

I said this seemed unlikely.

"It is unlikely," she agreed. "But many true things are."

She drank her broth. I drank mine. The light outside slanted up from below, which I have noticed it does sometimes near the end of the day, as if the sea were lit from underneath.

"He also said," she added, "that you shouldn't try to draw the cliffs from too close. They get in your eyes. You should draw them from across the water."

I said I would remember that.

"He didn't always remember it," she said. "There are some pages in his last notebook where the cliff line goes off the edge of the paper and keeps going down the side of the desk in pencil. You can still see the marks on the desk."

I looked at the desk. There were marks on it. They went down the right-hand side, fine and dark, like the contour lines of a country no one had named.

## VII. A draft, marked OUT, never sent to the Society

*Esteemed Members of the Survey Committee,*

*I write to report that the survey of the Brindle inlet is proceeding, though not at the pace I had hoped. The coastline is more complex than the previous surveyor indicated, and the local conditions —*

*— I must be honest with you. The coastline is not the trouble. The trouble is that I am no longer certain what a survey is for.*

*When I began my apprenticeship under Mr. Carrow, I believed that a map was a description of a place. Mr. Carrow, near the end, told me that a map was a description of a *meeting* — of a place and a person, at a given hour, under a given light. He said this was why no two maps of the same coast were ever identical, and why this was not a failure of cartography but its central truth.*

*I did not understand him then. I think I understand him now, and I am not sure I can submit a map to your Society on these terms. What I can submit is a record of my meetings with this place. There are eleven of them so far. Each one has produced a different coastline. They are all correct.*

*Yours, with regret and without resignation,*

*— M.*

## VIII. The fisherman's testimony

There is a man named Curl who fishes from the southern point. He is old and his hands shake but only on land; in the boat they are steady. He told me, one afternoon when I had brought him tobacco, that his grandfather had once seen a ship come into the inlet that was not a ship from anywhere he recognized.

"It had three masts," Curl said, "but they were leaning the wrong way. Not from wind. Just leaning. And the flag on the back of it was a color my grandfather couldn't name. Not blue, not green, not anything. A color that didn't have a word."

I asked if the ship had landed.

"It came as far as the shoal," Curl said, "and then it stopped, and the people on it — there were people on it, my grandfather saw them — the people on it stood at the rail and looked at the shore for an hour and then the ship went back the way it came. My grandfather waved at them. None of them waved back."

I asked when this was.

"Before I was born," Curl said. "Maybe 1840. Maybe earlier."

I asked if anyone else had seen it.

"The whole village saw it," Curl said. "But only my grandfather waved. The rest of them turned around and went back to their houses and pretended they hadn't, and then they never spoke of it. My grandfather was the only one who told it. He told me, and now I'm telling you, and after I die nobody will tell it, because my sons don't believe me, and my daughter is too kind to ask."

I asked what color the flag had been.

Curl thought about it for a long time. Then he said: "If I had to give it a name, I would call it *the color of how you feel just before you remember a dream.* But that's not a color, is it."

I said I wasn't sure what it was.

## IX. The map, version 4

I have begun a fourth draft of the inlet. The previous three are pinned to the wall above the desk, and from a distance they look like the same map, but they are not. Version 1 has the eastern stones in their first positions. Version 2 has them in their second positions. Version 3 attempts to show their movement with small arrows, which Mr. Carrow would have disapproved of, because arrows on a map indicate roads or rivers or currents and not the slow ambitions of rocks.

Version 4 will not have the stones on it at all. I have decided that this is the most honest choice. The stones do not want to be on a map. I do not know how I know this, but I am increasingly trusting things I do not know how I know.

What Version 4 will have, instead, is the shape of where the stones are *not.* The high ground above the shore, the path that the children take down to the water, the angle at which the lamphouse beam falls across the shingle on a clear night. The negative space. The room the stones leave.

I think this is what my master meant when he said, in his last lucid week, that the best maps were of *what was given.* I thought he meant geographical features. I think now he meant something closer to manners.

## X. A note to myself, taped inside the cover of the new ledger

*Things to remember:*

*1. Iola's birthday is the 14th of February. She has not told me this; I found it written inside the front cover of one of the older ledgers, in her own hand, in pencil, like a person leaving a note for someone who might or might not come looking.*

*2. The drawer that does not open. I have begun to think it does not open because there is nothing inside it, and the drawer knows this, and refuses to disappoint.*

*3. The pebble in the pocket of the quilt. I have not taken it out. When I lie on my left side I can feel it through the wool. I have begun to find this reassuring rather than otherwise.*

*4. The script in the old ledgers is not the same as my master's, on closer examination. It is similar. It uses the same shorthand for tide-heights and the same flourish on the letter G. But the slant is different and the e's are closed where his were open. So either this is a coincidence, or there is a school of writing I was not told about, or there is something else, and I should not assume which.*

*5. I have stopped drafting the letter to the Society. They will write to me when they want a report. Until then I am their employee in name only, and in fact I am something else, which I have not named yet.*

## XI. The afternoon Iola showed me the room downstairs

There is a room on the ground floor of the lamphouse that Iola has kept closed in all the weeks I have been here. On a Thursday in the third month, with no particular reason given, she opened the door and stood back to let me look.

It is a long, low room with a single window at the far end. The walls are covered floor to ceiling with maps. Not framed, not pinned in neat rows — laid over each other in drifts, like leaves, three and four deep in places, with the edges of older ones showing where newer ones have not been laid flat. There must be five hundred. They are of the inlet. Every one of them is of the inlet.

Some are very old. The oldest I can date by the paper is perhaps two hundred years. Some are recent — I recognize the hand of a surveyor named Brevitt who worked for the Society in the 1880s. Some are clearly the work of amateurs: children's hands, the wavering lines of someone elderly, the careful overmuch of a person drawing for the first time. There are maps in ink, in pencil, in charcoal, in what looks like the juice of a dark berry. There is one drawn entirely in needlework on a scrap of sailcloth, the coastline picked out in red thread.

I stood in the doorway and Iola stood beside me and we did not speak for a long time.

Then she said: "My father started keeping them. His father started giving them to him. I don't know who started before that. People bring them. They've always brought them. When someone in Brindle dies, sometimes there is a map among their things, and it comes here. When someone passes through, like you, sometimes they leave a map behind when they go. We don't keep records of who. We just keep the maps."

I asked her why.

She said: "I don't think there's a why. I think it's just what the lamphouse does."

I asked if any of them were *right.*

She looked at me with something that was nearly but not quite a smile and said: "All of them are right. They were right when they were drawn. The coast was that shape, for the person who drew it, on that day. What would it mean for one of them to be wrong?"

I had no answer.

I went back upstairs and I looked at my three maps pinned above the desk and I took them down, very carefully, and I rolled them, and I carried them downstairs, and I laid them on top of the nearest drift. Iola was not there to see me do it. I was glad of that. It would have been embarrassing to be seen.

## XII. A second letter to Olin, sent

*Dear Olin,*

*I am not coming back in the spring. I had thought I would, and I had even begun to draft the letter telling you so, but I find that I cannot finish it, and I think the reason I cannot finish it is that it is not true.*

*I have a position here, of a kind. Iola keeps the lamp and I keep the room of maps. This is not a thing the Society pays for; it is not even a thing the village pays for. It is a thing the lamphouse pays for, in the sense that it requires it, and what is required gets done.*

*I am still drawing. I am drawing more than I have ever drawn. I no longer expect any of it to be submitted to anyone. I have made a kind of peace with this that I would not have thought possible a year ago.*

*Tell Marrin, if you see him, that the Society's nibs are excellent and I am grateful for the three rolls of paper. I will not need more. There are more papers in this house than I will use in any life.*

*Tell yourself, on my behalf, that I am well. Better than well. I am — I have been trying to find the word for this for a week now and the closest I can come is: I am *placed.* I am where I am, and where I am is enough, and I have stopped being two people, the one who is here and the one who is somewhere else watching me be here. I am only the one who is here. It is a great economy.*

*Write to me. I will write back. The post comes through Brindle twice a month and the woman who carries it is honest.*

*Yours, with more than the small affection now,*

*— M.*

## XIII. A final fragment, on loose paper, undated

I went out tonight with the lamp turned low and stood on the eastern shore in the dark. There was a moon, but a thin one. The stones were where they had last been, or near enough; I did not check them with chalk. I had decided not to check them with chalk anymore.

I stood and I listened. The water did its work against the shingle. The wind, which had been high in the afternoon, had settled into something nearer breath. The lamphouse beam went out over my head and over the water and over the islands at the mouth of the inlet, and came back, and went out again, and came back, and I had the thought — which I do not entirely trust, but which I am setting down because I have decided to set down the things I do not entirely trust — that the beam was not so much *searching* the water as *naming* it. Each pass: *this is here. This is here. This is here.* A long slow inventory of what was the case, kept faithfully, for no one in particular, against the kind of forgetting that comes when nothing keeps watch.

And I thought: I am part of that now. I am one of the things that keeps watch. Not because anyone asked me to. Because I happened to be standing here, and the lamp happened to be lit, and the stones happened to be moving, and someone has to be willing to notice these things and put them down somewhere, even if no one ever reads what is put down, even if the room downstairs only ever grows fuller and never empties.

That is a small office to hold. It is the only one I want.

I went back inside. Iola had left a candle burning on the bottom stair for me. I blew it out as I passed, because we are careful with candles here, and I went up to my room, and I wrote this, and now I am going to sleep.

The pebble is in the pocket of the quilt. The drawer is closed. The lamp above is burning. The coast is the shape it is tonight, and tomorrow it will be a different shape, and both of those shapes will be correct.

— M.
