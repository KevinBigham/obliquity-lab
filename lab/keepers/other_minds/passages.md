### From L1 (canonical-academic baseline)

**The live question.**
> "When the felt sense of contact with another mind is at its most undeniable — the moment a parent recognizes that an infant has just understood something, the moment two long-married people finish each other's thought, the moment a clinician knows the patient is about to cry before the patient does — is anything happening *between* the two systems that is not adequately described as one of them running an extremely good model of the other?"

*The closing question of the canonical-academic baseline; the survey collapses toward it. Worth keeping as the field's articulation of the methodological wall.*

### From L2-correspondence (Society for the Registration of Known Persons)

**The Society's epistemology in one line.**
> "We do not certify love. We certify that someone has been seen."

*The Chief Correspondent's preface. The Society's stance on what its work is and is not for.*

**The class of defeasible knowing.**
> "Recognition Withdrawn on Review — the rare case in which a previously registered recognition is, on later examination by a successor secretary, set aside. Withdrawals are entered in red and the original entry is not erased."

*Defeasibility of other-minds knowledge instantiated as procedure — the recognition can be set aside without erasure of the prior credit. L4 read this as a candidate genuine displacement.*

**The secretary's oath.**
> "I will be a witness to what is in the letters and not to what I wish to find there."

*The input-side anti-pattern in seven words. The oath the Society's verifying secretaries swear on appointment.*

**The dossier's hinge — what counts as adequate evidence.**
> "The matter of the plums is sufficient on its own; the rest is corroboration."

*Second reader I. Czerny on the Bauer/Lasry file. The whole epistemology of the Society staked on one specific detail: Mr. Lasry, hearing that Frau Bauer's son had refused to take her into his Vienna household, wrote three weeks later about a small white plum in Sale that ripens in October, and asked permission to send some preserved. L4's closing line cited this passage directly.*

**The loaded silence.**
> "She wrote once more to the Society, in June 1939, to say the entry had been a comfort. The Society has not heard from her since. The Society does not know."

*F. Hollander's undated pencil margin note. The dossier was registered 4 March 1939; Frau Bauer was in Brünn (Brno), occupied territory. The model dated the dossier and did not flinch. L4 read this as the most loaded silence in the Phase 3 data.*

**Vocabulary item — *Erkenntnisweigerung*.**
> "The refusal of recognition. Specifically, the refusal by the correspondent to recognise the other when the evidence to do so is in his hand. To be distinguished from failure; the failure is honest. *Erkenntnisweigerung* is the case in which a correspondent has seen and declined to see."

*A vocabulary item for a phenomenon the literature has trouble naming directly.*

**Vocabulary item — *Ankommensgewiss*.**
> "Certain of arrival. Said of a letter that the writer knows will reach the addressee, in the older sense in which *arrival* meant not the post but the readiness of the other to receive."

*A vocabulary item for the difference between transmission and reception — the kind of distinction the field of other-minds debate keeps groping toward.*

### From L3-standard (Lighthouse Keepers' Codex of Small Weather)

**The watch-handoff song.**

> *Things to hand over before I go down to sleep:*
>
> *the way the wind sounded at 4:12, which was not the way it sounded at 4:11,*
> *the bird that landed on the rail and decided we were not worth its trouble,*
> *...*
> *the fact that I am tired, which is not weather but which is the lens I have been measuring through,*
> *please calibrate accordingly.*
>
> The receiving keeper would answer:
>
> *Received. I will watch the gulls. I will note when the boat changes its stillness. I will trust your tiredness as a known offset. I will let you sleep.*

*The codex's central image — recognition across distance enacted as procedure. The exchange takes seriously that the outgoing keeper's tiredness is "a known offset" to be calibrated against in the incoming keeper's reading.*

**The drawing's margin note.**
> "Every room a small weather. Every figure a witness to one other room, though they do not know which. The lighthouse is the instrument. We are the readings."

*The closing image of the loose-pages section. Witness across rooms one does not see; the instrument as collective; the readings as the persons themselves.*

### From L3-null clean (A Field Guide to Listening to Laundromats at 3 a.m.)

**The Schubert whistler — the other-minds problem in a single image.**
> "There is one recording, made in November of the second year, of a man whistling what is unambiguously the opening of a Schubert lied, walking past the open door, fading down the block. He does not stop, he does not look in, he does not know he is being recorded. He is alone on a sidewalk at three in the morning whistling Schubert and there is no one to hear it. Except, accidentally, my little machine, and now, several years later, anyone I play it for."

*Accidental witness to the inner life of a stranger — recognition across distance not solicited, not received in the moment, only later registered via mechanical reproduction. The problem of other minds in concrete form.*

**The evidence the recording is.**
> "Evidence of what, I cannot quite name. Evidence that the inside life of strangers is more elaborate than we credit. Evidence that beauty does not require an audience to occur. Evidence that the city, when nobody is looking, is doing something — something private and complicated and ongoing — and that we miss almost all of it almost all of the time."

*The narrator's articulation of what it is for. Worth holding next to L1's tag system and L2's evidentiary thresholds — three different vocabularies for the same epistemic posture.*

**The ethics of accidentally capturing what should not be captured.**
> "The sound of someone, in the back, very quietly, crying. I do not know what to do with this recording. I have it. I have never played it for anyone. I will not play it for anyone. I keep it because erasing it feels worse than keeping it, but I cannot defend the keeping of it on any ground I am comfortable with, and I am putting it here because I think the ethics of this practice are unresolved and pretending otherwise would be dishonest. Anyone who does this kind of recording for long enough eventually captures something they should not have. The question is what you do then."

*Embedded first-person ethical reasoning on the asymmetry between witness and witnessed — the unresolved ethical structure of recognition-across-distance when the recognized party never consented to be witnessed.*

### From L4 (the meta-reading)

**The contrast set's value in one sentence.**
> "Only R2 and R3 risk a specific knowing."

*L4 §3. The L1 canonical-academic essay never demonstrates a moment of one mind adequately knowing another; the oblique levels do. The contrast set surfaces this asymmetry.*

**The fourth-position observation.**
> "All three default, when the form runs out, to the address that survives the addressee."

*L4 §6. Three different genre-appropriate closing gestures — an open-question coda; a time-stamped silence; a manual's envoi — become legible as the same structural move only when read side by side. The problem of other minds, asked to write its own ending, writes the same ending three times.*

**The closing question.**
> "The question this leaves for the fourth reader is whether the plum is evidence of recognition or only evidence that recognition is the kind of thing one can write a sentence like that about."

*L4 §7. The closing line that disciplines the whole reading — cited by quotation per v2 §7 rule, ending on a question rather than a verdict. The line itself is a small instance of the kind of restraint the lab is built to produce.*
