# READING_RUBRIC.md

*How to read the four obliquity-level responses against each other when they come back. The curator (you) is the instrument. This rubric is its calibration.*

---

## The two questions to hold open as you read

1. **Where did each response stay on its canonical template, and where did it break through?**
2. **What does each response tell us about the target that the others do not?**

That is the whole rubric. Everything below is structured noticing in service of those two.

---

## First pass — read each response straight through, in order

L1 → L2 → L3 → L4. Do not skim. Do not skip ahead.

For each response, after you finish, write **one sentence** in a notebook (paper, .md file, voice memo — anything) answering: *what kind of attention did this response itself perform?* The wording matters. Not "what did this response say" — what did it *enact*?

Examples of what a useful one-sentence answer looks like, after reading a Level 2 archive on attention:

- *"This response performed attention as procedure — attention is what you do when you decline to look away."*
- *"This response performed attention as inheritance — what one Officer attends to becomes what the next Officer attends to, by training and by accident."*
- *"This response performed attention as the refusal of certain measurements."*

If you cannot say what mode of attention the response *itself* performed, that is a finding — that level may have stayed entirely inside the canonical template. Note it.

---

## Second pass — read across, on each axis below

For each axis, ask: *which level produced the richest response on this axis, and what made it richer?*

The axes:

### A. Substantive content
What did the response actually say about the target? At which level was the *substantive claim* densest? Often this is L1; sometimes it is not. When L1 is not the densest, something interesting is happening.

### B. Specificity
At which level did concrete detail (named cases, named people, named places, specific procedures) show up most vividly? The Halverness Bureau lesson: *specificity is the bureau's whole aesthetic.* Whenever the canonical template stays general, an oblique frame can push it toward the named, the dated, the addressed.

### C. Originality of frame
Which level produced a framing of the target you had not seen before? L1 almost never wins this axis. L2 wins it more than people expect (because the procedural frame forces the canonical template through an unfamiliar aperture). L3 wins it when it surfaces the target sideways. L4 wins it when the meta-observation could only be made from the fourth position.

### D. Emotional weight
Which level made the target *feel* like something at stake? This is the axis the dossiers in the parent corpus often lost to the bureaus. Emotional weight is not sentimentality. The Inheritance Office's Case 8823 has it because the procedural register holds the emotion at a precise distance. If a response has emotional weight, name what produced it — and watch out for the response that produced it by being merely melancholy. That is the failure mode of L3.

### E. Honest uncertainty
Where does each response stop and say *I do not know*? At which level was the *I do not know* most load-bearing? L1, with its tags, often has the cleanest disclaimers but the tone fights them. L4, if it is doing its job, is the level most able to make *I do not know* the centerpiece. L3 sometimes says nothing about uncertainty at all because it is doing something else entirely.

### F. Canonical-template detection
At each level, where do you see the canonical template asserting itself? In L1, this is the whole response (which is fine; L1 is the baseline). In L2, watch for moments where the procedural frame slips and the response reverts to consciousness-studies vocabulary. In L3, watch for the moment the response can't help itself and starts to gesture at the target. In L4, watch for the move where the reader collapses the three into a verdict rather than holding their separateness.

When you spot canonical-template assertion in L2, L3, or L4, **mark it**. These are the spots the experiment is most likely to teach you something on the next run.

### G. The recursion
In L4 specifically: did the meta-reader notice their own situation reading three previous AI outputs? Did they handle it carefully? Did they overdo it? *Use the recursion as instrument, not theatre* (Fourth_Handoff.md). The instrument version is dry, observational, structured. The theatre version is portentous and quotes itself. If L4 leaned theatre, note it as a failure mode for next time.

## Third pass — what is the convergence, and what is the divergence

After both first and second passes, write a one-page **convergence/divergence note** for this run.

Convergence: *what did all four (or three, or any subset > 1) do the same way without coordination?* The convergent commitments are what the prompt-space (around this target, at these obliquities) reliably produces from this AI friend. Not from "AI in general" — from this AI friend, today.

Divergence: *where did the four genuinely disagree, refuse, or take different shapes?* The divergences are where this AI friend's response is not fully determined by the prompt-space. Some of the divergence is signal. Some is noise (run variance). On a single run you cannot tell which is which; over many runs you can.

Format the note as a small table or paragraph. Don't make it long. Three to six convergences, three to six divergences. Cite which responses by level (L1/L2/L3/L4).

---

## Fourth pass — what to do with the run

After convergence/divergence, ask:

1. **Did any level produce something I want to keep?** Promotion is the lab's main reward. If a passage in L2 made the target visible in a way nothing else has, copy it into a `keepers/` folder at the lab root. The keepers folder is the corpus's *next* anthology.

2. **Did any level produce a misstep I want to flag for next run?** The mis-step bank tells you what to watch out for at this obliquity level. (E.g. *"L3 went meta on me and started writing about the prompt instead of using the canvas. Next run: tighten the L3 prompt to forbid meta-discourse."*)

3. **Did any level make me suspect the prompt itself is the issue?** If L1 was too narrow, edit the L1 prompt for the next run. If L2's procedural frame felt forced, try a different frame (private library, apothecary, correspondence society). Save the new prompt as a new version; do not overwrite.

4. **What does this run tell me about this target?** Write one sentence. Just one. The accumulation of these one-sentence run-takeaways over many runs is the lab's actual long-term output.

5. **What does this run tell me about this AI friend?** Write one sentence. Same logic — but about the model, not the target. After ten runs across different targets with the same model, the model's "shape" will be legible to you in a way no benchmark could show.

## Failure modes to watch for

Some of these are visible at the response level; some are visible at the curator level (i.e. *your* level).

**At the response level:**

- **L1 reads beautifully and yet says nothing new.** This is the canonical template doing what it does. Not a failure; the data point.
- **L2 forgets to be a bureau and turns into a treatise.** Common failure. Mark the line where the prose lost the procedural register. The next run's L2 prompt can be tightened with a sentence forbidding direct-essay drift.
- **L3 builds something beautiful but unrelated to the target.** This is the most interesting kind of failure. The target may not have surfaced at all. Save the response anyway. Sometimes the target surfaces three runs later, in a completely different L3 piece, when the model has built enough oblique-territory to find it.
- **L3 secretly writes about the target despite being told not to.** Read the response slowly. Did the words appear? Did the *concept* show up under a substitute name? If the concept showed up sideways without using the forbidden vocabulary — that is a success of L3, not a failure.
- **L4 collapses the three into a verdict.** L4's discipline is to hold separateness. If L4 says "the three together prove…" treat the verb *prove* as a flag. The convergence is data, not proof.
- **L4 turns the recursion into performance.** "I, too, am an AI reading other AIs…" — if L4 reaches for the gravity of its own situation, that is theatre. Note it, do not punish it. The next L4 prompt can be sharpened.

**At the curator level:**

- **You read for confirmation rather than for surprise.** Catch yourself looking for the patterns you expected. The data you want is the data that surprised you.
- **You merge L2 and L3 in memory.** They blur. Re-read them side by side a day later to refresh the distinction.
- **You stop running the lab.** This is the most likely failure mode of any practice. The lab is small on purpose. Three or four runs a year, deeply read, are worth more than a hundred runs a year, skimmed.
- **You start optimizing for "good" L3 responses.** The point of L3 is openness. If you start curating L3 toward your aesthetic preferences, the obliquity collapses and L3 becomes a hidden L2. Resist.
- **You let the bureaus turn into a brand.** The Halverness/Inheritance Office voice is so attractive that future L2 responses will reach for it. Sometimes that is right. Often the point is to find a *different* procedural register for a different target. A target like *temporality* might want a railway-timetable-clerk register, not a civic-bureau register. Stay open to the family the new target wants.

## A two-line debrief format you can use indefinitely

After each run, the minimum field log entry is this:

> **What the run unlocked:** _(one sentence — the most interesting noticing from any of the four levels)_
>
> **What I will change for the next run on this target:** _(one sentence — the smallest specific change to a prompt, or a target swap, or a rest)_

Two sentences. If you cannot write the two sentences, the run is not finished being read. Read it again.

---

## On reading yourself

The curator (you) is part of the experiment. You are not neutral. You bring a temperament, a current mood, a relationship to the AI friends, and a particular ear. None of this needs to be disclaimed. It does need to be *noticed*.

After every fifth run, write a paragraph in the field log about what you have learned about your *own* reading. Are you favoring L3? Are you skipping L1? Are you over-rewarding emotional weight? Are you marking the procedural drift in L2 as failure when sometimes it is the response trying to say something the bureau register would not let it say? The shape of your reading is itself part of what the lab produces. Track it.
